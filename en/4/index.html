Chapter 3 Black Cod Stéphanie fell asleep early that evening. Jacques opened an Omnipollo beer, then a second, and finally a third, without finding in them the taste of rest. Around midnight, he went to join her in the bedroom and slipped in beside her. On Saturday, they got up late. Jacques suggested a walk in Djurgården before nightfall. “Come on. It’ll take your mind off things.” Stéphanie’s eyes, usually so full of laughter, opened all at once with a childlike joy. Jacques felt his heart grow warm. They walked for a long time without aim through the royal park. The trees were bare. The grass was white. From time to time, Stéphanie stopped facing the sea. They went into a small café. Stéphanie smiled, then her face closed again, barely, with a tiny crease at the corner of her eye. She had received a message. She looked at the screen without opening it straight away, then placed the phone face down on the table. “Is it your daughter?” Jacques asked. Stéphanie nodded. “She’s better,” she said, without conviction. “She’s stopped crying. But she asked me if I was coming back. Twice.” Jacques felt powerless. He thought of the father’s sentence, of the vile nature of his little attack. He said nothing. They continued walking. Parents were pulling sledges. Children laughed, sending sharp cries into the white air. Stéphanie watched them, and Jacques had the impression that she was also looking at something else, a fulfilled family life she might have had, or had believed she possessed. On the way back, they bought a few things at the supermarket. Jacques bought salmon, dark bread and fruit. In the apartment, he put the bags on the worktop, switched on a lamp, and the room suddenly made itself welcoming. Stéphanie took off her jumper. She went into the bathroom and washed her hands for a long time. Jacques watched her without interrupting. Then she sat on the sofa, her legs folded beneath her, the phone on her knees. She sent a message, received another, and replied again. When at last she put the phone down, Stockholm had already sunk into darkness. She closed her eyes for a moment. “I’m sorry,” she said. “I’m useless.” Jacques shook his head. “You’re tired. That’s all.” She opened her eyes again and caught his gaze. Between them, there was a second of silence. Jacques moved closer. He sat down beside her, without touching her at first. He felt his own body, desire, the fear of doing the wrong thing. Stéphanie placed her hand on his with a cautious gesture. She had that composed face he knew, the face of an adult who refuses to complain. Jacques kissed her. She did not respond at once. Then, slowly, she loosened. Her body drew nearer with hesitation. They went towards the bedroom without speaking. Jacques was afraid of breaking something, and that apprehension made him gentler. In the bedroom, Stéphanie stopped beside the bed. She looked for a moment at the window, at the night, then turned towards him. Jacques undid the buttons of her shirt. He did not hurry. He touched her with careful slowness. Beneath his fingers, he felt the tension give way in places, return, then yield again. She closed her eyes, and a deeper breath passed through her body. When they came together, it was a return. Jacques felt the warmth rise again, the fierce love of summer, but transformed, less impatient. There was something recovered in it, and something new. She clung to him with a silent strength. They remained like that for a long time, until fatigue caught up with them. Jacques felt the sweat on his skin, the crumpled sheet, and the distant noise of the city. Stéphanie got up first. She returned with a glass of water. She set it on the bedside table, then sat on the edge of the bed. She gently stroked Jacques’s hair. The gesture was simple. And yet he felt a discreet shock as he thought again of the club he had not told her about. Stéphanie slipped under the blanket and remained motionless for a while, her eyes open in the dark. She was going to speak. He did not dare help her towards it. He waited. “I’m going to tell you something,” she said at last. “I never explained to you why my husband and I separated.” Jacques did not answer. He simply placed his hand on her thigh. Stéphanie drew breath. “I thought it was a classic story of betrayal. I thought I had been cheated on, that was all. I thought there was another woman, that the love between us had withered after the birth of our son. I told myself these things happen. I would have cried, I would have been humiliated, but I would have survived.” She gave a small laugh, full of melancholy. “But it wasn’t that.” She was looking at the ceiling. The room was dark, and yet Jacques had the impression he could see her very clearly. He held his breath. “One evening, his phone lit up in the dark. I simply glimpsed a notification. I picked it up, without thinking. And I read.” She broke off. “I saw a man’s name. Underneath it there was a sentence that was too intimate. I read it three times. Not because I doubted it. Because my brain could not understand what it was reading.” Jacques felt his throat tighten. Stéphanie continued in the same voice, gentle and precise. “I didn’t make a scene. I didn’t even shout. The floor gave way beneath my feet. And I felt an immense sadness at the thought that I had spent all that time beside a stranger.” She turned her head towards Jacques. Her eyes were shining, but she was not crying. “What finished me was that everything about him made sense. He came from Poitou, like me. He had climbed the social ladder. He had given us that way of life that silences doubt: the large flat near Bayswater, on Moscow Road, holidays in Porquerolles, the bilingual school for our children. I had taken that for proof of love and solidity. I believed solidity could replace the rest.” She closed her eyes for a moment. “He had been leading a double life for years. It hadn’t been an affair. It hadn’t been an accident. The construction was perfect. The lie had patiently settled in.” Jacques’s mouth went dry. Stéphanie went on, and her voice became more precise. “He even invited his lover to our home one evening, passing him off as a colleague. I nodded along. I did what I always do. I tried to make everything right.” A silence passed. Then she said, very softly: “I cooked black cod marinated in miso and sake. You know, the Nobu recipe.” Jacques looked at her. “All evening, I smiled. I wanted to be perfect. I wanted the guest to feel welcome, for my husband to be proud of me.” She swallowed. “And they had those silences. They exchanged quick smiles. Their eyes lingered. I did feel it that evening, without understanding. I cleared up afterwards. I washed the glasses and the plates. Like an idiot.” A first tear finally ran down her face. She did not wipe it away. “When I read the messages later, it all came back to me at once. And I understood that I had not merely cooked. I had set the stage. I had prepared the scenery of my own humiliation.” Stéphanie resumed, more quietly: “It isn’t that he cheated on me. It’s that he… he never loved me the way I thought he did. He loved a façade. A normality. He played that part. And I, in the end, loved alone.” At last she turned her face towards Jacques. Tears were running down her cheeks. She was sobbing. “Since then, I don’t know how to trust any more. Even when I want to. Even when I love you.” Jacques took her into his arms. He did not speak. He did not search for the sentence that would put things right. He simply held her. Stéphanie rested her forehead against his shoulder. Her breathing broke for a second, then returned. She murmured: “Tonight, I tried.” Jacques kissed her hair. In the dark, he heard her phone vibrate, a distant notification. He did not move. Stéphanie drew back a little, just enough to look at him. “Will you promise me one thing?” she asked. Jacques nodded. “Will you promise me that if, one day, you don’t want to go on, you’ll tell me?” He answered, without emphasis: “I’ll tell you.”
