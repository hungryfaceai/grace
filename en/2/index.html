PART ONE “Think of the sweetness Of going there to live together!” Charles Baudelaire, Invitation to the Voyage Chapter 1 The Consensus On Monday morning, Jacques woke at seven. He drank three cups of coffee while looking out at the frozen water from the sitting room of the sumptuous three-room apartment he had rented in Östermalm, facing Nybroviken. He thought about the week ahead. The phone vibrated on the coffee table, screen against the wood, like a small impatient animal. Jacques turned it over. Stéphanie had written early, from London. “Did you do anything this weekend? Did you go out?” He remained for a moment looking at the question, so simple. He thought of the club, of the handkerchief above all, and of the unease he had felt rising in him. He had the absurd desire to tell her everything, to unburden himself. He typed: “I went…” Then he deleted it. He typed again: “Not much.” He hesitated once more, added a sentence, deleted that too. In the end he sent: “I kept quiet. I miss you.” When the message went, his shoulders dropped. He put the phone away. He told himself he would tell her later. He put on his long coat, his gloves and his hat, and went out. He strode down Strandvägen, caught in the February cold. He hurried towards the office. Since his arrival at the Swedish investment bank where he had signed on for a year, he had been asked his opinion on everything. No decision seemed complete until it had been “validated” by an Anglo-Saxon consultant paid a fortune. Most of them worked no more than two hours a day, with the solemnity of priests serving a godless cult. Jacques hung up his coat, got another coffee, opened his laptop. In his inbox, three invitations to “catch-ups” had piled up overnight. He accepted them without reading them, out of reflex. In his mind he arranged the chairs for the weekly meeting. He already knew half the team would be missing. The English and the Germans arrived on Mondays as one joins a distant war, in waves, from London and Frankfurt, between eleven o’clock and four. The last, coming from Luxembourg, indulged, or rather was indulged in, the luxury of starting his week at five in the afternoon, invoking the absence of a direct flight. Jacques sometimes even crossed paths with him at the entrance, while, in the lift, Swedes were already descending towards their homes, with the peaceful air of those who had finished their day. The head of the department had made her decision: the meeting would remain at nine o’clock. She had briefly considered organising one for every hour of the day, then had given up the idea. At nine, Jacques joined his Swedish colleagues in the meeting room. They were talking about their weekend of cross-country skiing with their families, children bundled into multicoloured snowsuits, hauled in little sledges until exhaustion. The head of the department was called Alma. On the first day, Jacques had thought she must once have been very beautiful. After thirty-four years in banking, she ran IT research and development with the dry energy of survivors, that way of standing upright in the middle of ruins. She began, as always, soberly. “We need a consensus.” Consensus, in this bank, had the status of a mythological creature. Everyone spoke of it, no one had ever seen it, and as soon as one approached its natural habitat, it vanished into the undergrowth of “dependencies” and “unvalidated budget.” Slides were shown. Someone asked what “consensus” meant exactly in the context. Someone else pointed out that the budget had not been validated. A third person spoke of dependencies with the gravity of an addiction specialist. Voices overlapped, then died away, then returned, each defending its small piece of territory. Jacques watched the hands: those playing with a pen, those smoothing the corner of a sheet of paper, those typing a message beneath the table. The consensus did not occur. It never occurred. Jacques had already stopped listening. The evening from two days earlier came back to him in fragments: the skin, the dimmed light, and the handkerchief, above all. He preferred not to share his thoughts with the team. Alma would do perfectly well without his ideas. Jacques remembered that the colleague sitting next to him had almost strangled another the previous week, and he nodded along to each of his sentences with prudent agreement. Independent consultants like him collected, as a bonus, a more muted animosity, as though their mere presence reminded everyone, quietly, of the failure to manage without them. An hour later, each person left the meeting hating a little more those with whom he had failed to agree. Alma, for her part, had the satisfying impression of having made progress, which is to say of having moved, without resolving, the same problems from one week to the next. At eleven in the morning, the Swedish colleagues went out in clusters to have lunch in the basement. Jacques had declined the invitation on the first day; it was never renewed. The office emptied. A kind of peace settled in. Jacques sat on a sofa, took out his iPhone 7 and gave himself over to his favourite pastime: sliding his index finger from left to right, as quickly as possible. From time to time, a woman’s face appeared: smiling promise, first name, quotation. Jacques pasted the same message each time, with the quiet efficiency of a diligent employee. After twenty-five minutes, the profiles ran dry. The screen flickered, then offered up sidereal emptiness. There were no more candidates available for the moment. He placed the phone face down on the sofa. He went out to walk as far as Kungsträdgården. Outside, the air cut into him. He came back an hour later, sat at his desk and resumed work with impeccable seriousness. It was two o’clock; most of his English colleagues had just settled in at their workstations. Doctor Fynn came over to him. “How are you, Jacques? Did you stay in Stockholm over the weekend? What did you do?” Jacques nodded. “Not much,” he replied vaguely. “My partner, Stéphanie, is divorced. She had to look after her children. She stayed in London, as she often does. She didn’t come over.” “How did the meeting go this morning? Did you speak to Alma about my new model?” “No,” Jacques replied. He had forgotten. Fynn was a renowned mathematician, one of those who had managed to convert a solid academic gift into a lucrative career. He had made his fortune implementing statistical trading algorithms inside a New York hedge fund. Back in London, he had treated himself to a splendid Edwardian house in Hammersmith, facing the Thames, beneath the continuous rumble of planes approaching Heathrow Airport from four in the morning onwards. As he spoke, he worried at his tie with methodical nervousness, to the point that it had become worn. He wore trousers that were too short, exposing a pair of battered Tod’s, quite unsuited to Stockholm snow; whenever he went outside, he covered them with little transparent plastic caps that greatly amused Jacques. His work consisted of giving a price to the future. He called this a model. He tried to capture unstable quantities, contaminated by man, by his panics, his greed and his tricks. He assumed uncertainty obeyed a law. For a long time, Jacques had believed in the usefulness of his profession. He was no longer so sure. Jacques recalled London, eight years earlier. He had worked on assets linked to credit risk. He had been astonished to see the systems developed by the bank’s researchers produce such extravagant valuations. Behind the screens there had been a false serenity. One day, he had taken the lift to go and speak to Research. “Who are you? Ah, the frog downstairs? Next time, make an appointment. Are you a PhD? Don’t try to understand. Don’t even try.” Jacques had gone back down to the basement. He had nevertheless tried to understand, a little, tapping away on his first-generation iPhone. Copula, Google had replied affably. He had continued coding. Six months later, he had gone upstairs to present his system to the credit desk. They had made him wait. He had asked where his users were. No one seemed to know. He insisted, politely. Then he came upon an obese man in his thirties, a cardboard box in one hand, a beer in the other. His shirt strained across his belly. “Do you know where the credit desk sits?” Jacques had politely enquired. “You’re late, mate. I’m the last one. And I’ve just been fired too.” He had smiled as he said it, without bitterness, with that thoroughly British good nature. He shrugged, mocking, and added: “Pack up your software. Get a box. There are some over there.” And Jacques had seen himself, for an instant, standing there with his computer, his seriousness, and his useless equations, in the middle of a collapse that was simply called “restructuring.”
