# Fragile Grace ## 1. Prologue: The Handkerchief Jacques had been walking for twenty minutes through the streets of Stockholm when he realized he was lost. The streets of Södermalm looked alike, the facades remained mute, while a fine snow fell without a sound. He checked the address on his phone one last time. Nothing indicated the entrance. He hesitated, retraced his steps, and finally found a door. The air inside was warm. The heat carried perfumes, alcohol, the smell of skin. The music throbbed, not very loudly, but with an obstinate regularity, like a heart trying to imitate life. Jacques took off his coat and handed it to a woman who smiled back at him. He had no social appetite, but forced himself to cross the first room. Women and men moved about slowly. He recognized the dim light, the laughter, the bursts of conversation, the glances offered and withdrawn, and that strange courtesy of desire when it becomes collective. Jacques spotted the bar, then, at the back, a second room. He exchanged a few words in Swedish and took the flute of champagne that was handed to him. He settled near a loudspeaker, his back to the wall. He observed. He thought of London, of those nights when he had convinced himself he was not looking for vice. In London, the crowd was noisier, more festive, more hurried. Here, everything seemed restrained. The city outside seemed still to be watching bodies through the walls. He wandered, moved into another room, then came back. He already felt tired. The week had seemed empty to him, as it often had since his arrival. He had signed a one-year contract. He worked for an investment bank. He passed through days from which he retained nothing. He was paid to wait. That was when he saw them. They occupied the center of a larger room, and the space seemed to arrange itself around them. A young man, perhaps in his twenties, possessed an insolent, almost mythological beauty. A tall blonde Swedish woman had a diaphanous delicacy. They were making love without haste. There was in their movements a quiet necessity. Around them, people watched the scene, some with a gentle avidity, others with an almost scientific curiosity. The young man, for his part, saw nothing. He was not performing. He was there, intent, fervent. For him, the world had narrowed to a patch of skin, a breath, a simple determination to live. The young woman unsettled Jacques by her face. That face spoke of trust, surrender, something that resembled love. In a place like that, such truth seemed almost indecent. They went on. Time loosened. They appeared to him like beings before the Fall, naked but without shame, strangers to the world around them. In that club saturated with eyes, they possessed, by some miracle, a scandalous innocence. Jacques felt a cold jealousy rising within him before the obvious bond that united them, that peace at the very heart of flesh. He was not merely outside. He was already after it all, after the Fall, after the expulsion, condemned to desire without ever believing. What struck him was the sense of something divine in the secret accord between those two beings. He watched their gestures the way one watches, from the snow, a lit window, and he knew himself to be in exile. Then the young man withdrew, and their embrace came apart. Afterward, she took a handkerchief with ceremonial gentleness. First she wiped the sweat-drenched forehead of her lover, performing that simple, intimate, domestic gesture, a gesture of home in the middle of the spectacle. Then she knelt and wiped what had fallen onto the floor, without disgust, without haste, with calm attention. Cleaning became a way of loving. And only afterward, with the same cloth she folded back over itself, she wiped her own sex, gleaming and wet. Jacques remained motionless. The order of it struck him. He saw in it a tiny household liturgy. He recognized in it a way of arranging the world around another person, of protecting him, of taking care of him even down to his traces. The music went on. Conversations resumed around them. He swallowed the rest of his champagne. The liquid seemed lukewarm. He set the glass down. Then he turned around, retrieved his coat, thanked someone mechanically, and went out. Outside, the snow had begun again. A white dust settled patiently onto the pavements, the facades, the motionless cars. The cold bit him. The warmth of the club vanished at once. His footsteps made a dull sound in the snow. He took out his phone. The screen lit his fingers. For a moment he stood looking at that small, ridiculous light in the middle of the black city. ## 2. Consensus On Monday morning Jacques woke at seven. He drank three cups of coffee while looking at the sea from the living room of the sumptuous three-room apartment he had rented in Östermalm, facing Nybroviken. The windows, too large, gave the water the dignity of a witness. He thought about the week ahead. The phone vibrated on the coffee table, screen against the wood, like a small impatient animal. Jacques turned it over. Stéphanie had written early, from London. “Did you do anything this weekend? Did you go out?” He looked at the question for a moment, so simple in itself. He thought of the club, of that handkerchief which had awakened in him the jealousy of a penitent. He felt the absurd urge to tell her everything, to unburden himself, to purify himself. He typed, “I went...” Then he deleted it. He typed again, “Not much.” He hesitated once more, added a sentence, deleted that too. In the end he sent: “I took it easy. I miss you.” When the message went, his shoulders dropped. He put the phone away. He told himself he would explain later. He already knew he would explain nothing. He put on his long coat, his gloves, his wool hat, and went out. He strode down Strandvägen, caught in the glacial cold of February. He hurried toward his office, right in the center of the capital. Since his arrival at the Swedish investment bank where he had signed on for a year, people had been asking his opinion on everything, then thanking him with exaggerated solemnity. No decision seemed complete until it had been “validated” by an Anglo-Saxon consultant paid at a princely rate. Most of them worked no more than two hours a day, with the gravity of priests serving a godless cult. Jacques put down his coat, fetched another coffee, opened his computer. In his inbox three invitations to “catch-ups” had piled up overnight. He accepted them without reading, by reflex. In his mind he arranged the chairs for the weekly meeting. He already knew half the team would be missing. The English and the Germans arrived on Mondays as one joins a distant war, in waves, from London and Frankfurt, sometime between eleven and four. The last, who came from Luxembourg, indulged, or rather had indulged for him, the luxury of beginning his week at five in the evening, invoking the lack of a direct flight. It sometimes happened that Jacques crossed paths with him at the entrance while, in the elevator, Swedes were already descending homeward with the peaceful look of those who had finished their day. The head of department had decided the meeting would remain at nine. She had considered, briefly, organizing one every hour of the day, then abandoned the idea. At nine Jacques joined his Swedish colleagues in the meeting room. They were talking about their weekend cross-country skiing with the family, the children bundled in snowsuits, dragged in little sleds like projects one hauls along, smiling, to the point of exhaustion. The head of the department was called Alma. On the first day Jacques had thought she must once have been very beautiful. After thirty-four years in banking, she ran research and IT development with the dry energy of survivors, that way of staying upright in the middle of ruins. She began, as always, with a sobriety meant to reassure. “We need a consensus.” In that bank, consensus had the status of a mythological creature. Everybody spoke of it, nobody had ever seen it, and as soon as one approached its natural habitat it disappeared into the undergrowth of “dependencies” and “budget not validated.” Slides were shown. Someone asked what “consensus” meant exactly in the present context. Someone else remarked that the budget had not been validated. A third started speaking about dependencies with the same gravity as an addiction specialist. Voices overlapped, then died away, then returned, each defending its little patch of territory with cutting politeness. Jacques watched their hands: the ones fiddling with a pen, the ones smoothing the corner of a sheet of paper, the ones typing a message under the table. Consensus did not occur. It never occurred. Jacques was already no longer listening. The evening two days earlier came back to him in fragments: skin, muted light, and above all that handkerchief. He preferred not to share his thoughts with the team. Alma would manage very well without his ideas, he thought. Jacques remembered that the colleague sitting beside him had nearly strangled another one the previous week, and so he nodded prudently at every sentence he uttered. Independent consultants like him, as a bonus, attracted a murkier, less confessable hostility, as though their mere presence silently reminded everyone of the failure to manage without them. An hour later, everyone left the meeting hating a little more those with whom they had failed to agree. Alma, for her part, retained that satisfying impression of having moved things forward, which is to say of having shifted, without solving them, the same problems from one week to the next. At eleven in the morning the Swedish colleagues went downstairs in clusters for lunch in the basement. Jacques had declined the invitation on his first day; it was never renewed. The office emptied. A peace settled in. Jacques sat down on a sofa, took out his iPhone 7, and gave himself over to his favorite pastime: sliding his index finger from left to right as quickly as possible. Now and then a woman’s face appeared: smiling promise, first name, quotation. Jacques pasted the same message every time, with the quiet efficiency of a diligent employee, the way one fills out forms in the hope that one day the French administration might reply. After twenty-five minutes, the profiles ran dry. The screen flickered, then offered up the sidereal void. There were no more candidates available for the moment. He placed the phone face down on the sofa. He went out for a walk as far as Kungsträdgården. Outside, the air cut. The black branches held themselves against the pale sky. He returned an hour later, sat down at his workstation, and resumed work with impeccable seriousness. It was two in the afternoon; most of his English colleagues had just settled at their desks. Doctor Fynn came over to him. “How are you, Jacques? Did you stay in Stockholm over the weekend? What did you do?” Jacques nodded. “Not much,” he answered vaguely. “My partner, Stéphanie, is divorced. She had to look after her children. She stayed in London, as often happens. She didn’t join me.” “How did the meeting go this morning? Did you speak to Alma about my new model?” “No,” Jacques replied. He had forgotten. Fynn was a renowned mathematician, one of those who had managed to turn solid academic aptitude into a lucrative career. He had made a fortune by implementing statistical trading algorithms inside a New York hedge fund. Back in London, he had treated himself to a splendid Edwardian house in Hammersmith, facing the Thames, under the continuous rumble of planes coming in to land at Heathrow. When he spoke, he twisted his tie with methodical nervousness until it had worn thin. He wore trousers too short for him, exposing scuffed Tod’s shoes unsuited to the snow in Stockholm; every time he stepped outside, he wrapped them in little transparent plastic overshoes, which amused Jacques greatly. His work consisted in putting a price on the future. He called it a model. Jacques watched him speak and sometimes wondered whether it was not above all a polite way of organizing fear: fear of the unforeseen movement, of the day the market remembers that it does not obey equations. Fynn assumed uncertainty followed a law. For a long time Jacques had believed in the usefulness of his profession. He was no longer so sure. In the physical world, models describe an indifferent reality, one that repeats itself. Here, Fynn was trying to capture unstable quantities contaminated by man, by his panics, his greed, and his tricks. Models possessed the elegance of coherent lies. Jacques remembered London, eight years earlier. He had worked on assets linked to credit risk, the infamous CDOs that had despoiled widow, orphan, retiree, and invalid with mathematical elegance. He had been astonished to see the systems developed by the bank’s learned researchers produce such extravagant valuations. Behind the screens there was a false serenity. One day he had taken the lift to speak to research about it. “And who are you? Ah, the frog downstairs? Next time make an appointment. Are you a PhD? Don’t try to understand. Don’t even try.” Jacques had gone back down to the basement. He had tried to understand all the same, a little, tapping away on his first-generation iPhone. Copula, Google had answered him affably. He went on coding. Six months later he went upstairs to present his system to the credit desk. They made him wait. He asked where his users were. No one seemed to know. He insisted, politely. Then he came upon an obese man in his thirties, a cardboard box in one hand, a beer in the other. His shirt pulled across his stomach. “Do you know where the credit desk sits?” Jacques had inquired. “You’re late, mate. I’m the last one. I’ve just been fired.” He had said it with a smile, without bitterness, with that thoroughly British good nature. He shrugged, half mocking, and added, “Pack up your software. Grab a box. There are some over there.” And Jacques had seen himself for a moment standing there with his computer, his seriousness, and his useless equations, in the middle of a collapse that people referred to simply as “restructuring.” ## 3. Arrivals Hall Three days later, on Thursday evening, Jacques left the office at four. He picked up his computer, buttoned his coat, crossed the open-plan office with that mixture of discretion and haste which, in open-plan offices, arouses suspicion. In the lift he felt cheerful at the thought of being reunited with Stéphanie for a few days. Her children were with their father until Monday. Outside, night had already fallen. He hailed a Tesla taxi. The cabin smelled of new leather and cutting-edge technology. The driver asked no questions. Jacques merely said: Arlanda. They crossed Vasastan. Then the center receded. The facades lowered, the lights grew sparse, and the road unrolled ahead. Jacques took out his iPhone 7, then put it away again. He busied himself with absurd details: what he would say when he saw her, how he would place his hand on her suitcase, whether he should kiss her at once or wait. He had the same nervousness as before an important meeting, that habit of preparing sentences so as not to be caught off guard by emotion. He thought of London. Of last summer. London, in summer, is unlike itself. The city ceases for a moment to be a machine and becomes a setting again. People linger, pubs overflow, the parks turn into makeshift beaches. Even the light there seems softer, more indulgent. The memory of Stéphanie rose within him, tender and precise. Jacques had loved her passionately. Her scent, intoxicating and sensual, and that face marked by a silent kindness had seized him from the first encounter. She was raising two children alone in London, in a small flat overlooking Cleveland Square, with its carefully tended garden in the center, the sort one finds in the wealthy districts of west London. She had separated from her husband six months before meeting Jacques. He saw again their first night together. She had invited him to her place one summer evening, windows open over the little square garden, and they had watched *Leviathan*, that Russian film. He could see the sea again: a cold shore, rusted sheets of metal, and on the beach the white carcass of a whale. The story amounted to very little: a house too well situated, a coveted plot of land, and a man being told that what was his would be taken from him, in that tone some French notaries affect. They speak as though the state belonged to them, and burden the citizen with their indignity. Stéphanie had murmured, almost without moving, “They’re going to crush him.” Jacques had nodded. The court droned out its decision, the police smiled, friends drank, and the Church stood there, motionless. Then their bodies had begun to seek each other, and the film had gone on behind them. They had found each other at an age when one no longer searches, because one knows oneself well enough. Jacques was thirty-eight. She was forty. Beside her, he had known a peace, a fullness, of which he felt himself incapable. He knew she had left her husband a year earlier. He knew about the children, the logistics, the solitude. He knew too, through hints and silences, that the separation had been a detonation in Stéphanie’s life and in the children’s. But he did not know why. Stéphanie had never wanted to tell him. He had not pressed her, waiting for her to confide in him. The taxi was approaching Arlanda. The signs lined up, the exits followed one another. Jacques felt his stomach tighten. He was going to see her again, and the thought made him light. He would breathe in her suave scent when she came to nestle against him. In the arrivals hall, the warmth of the airport had something inhuman about it. Travelers slid past with rolling suitcases and tired eyes. Jacques took up position near the automatic doors. He waited. When she appeared, he did not recognize her at first. The journey, the fatigue, everything had made her thinner, more taut. She held her phone in one hand, her suitcase in the other. She searched for Jacques with her eyes, and when she saw him, something relaxed in her face, only for a second, then that reserve he had first noticed returned. They moved toward each other. He was about to say something awkward, one word too many, a needless joke. He opened his mouth, then fell silent. Stéphanie got there first. She laid a hand on his sleeve, a brief, sure, verifying touch, to make certain he was real. “I’m here,” she said. Jacques embraced her for a long time. He took her hand and they walked toward the exit. In the taxi on the way back they spoke about the weekend’s plans. Jacques proposed going skiing at Hellasgården. Stockholm flowed past, clean and black. Jacques looked at Stéphanie. She was staring at the road, her hands resting motionless on her bag. He felt the urge rise in him to touch her, to undo her, to bring her back to the body. Stéphanie breathed in, then out slowly. Jacques thought she was going to speak of her week. Instead she said, “I had my daughter on the phone. Just before boarding. She was crying.” Jacques moved closer to her. “What’s wrong?” Stéphanie hesitated for a second. Then she spoke in a controlled voice, without pathos, which made it harsher. “Their father told them... He told them, ‘Do you know where your mother is? She’s in Sweden this weekend. Anything could happen to you.’” She paused. “She’s seven. “She heard that and imagined... she imagined I was abandoning her, her and her little brother. That something might happen to them and I would not be there. She cried as though someone were pushing her into a hole.” Jacques felt a dry anger rising in him. He thought of the simple cruelty of adults, of the way children are used as weapons because it is more effective than an argument. He thought of what it must have cost Stéphanie to board that flight and come to join him. Stéphanie went on, more softly: “I kept repeating that I was coming back. That I wasn’t leaving them. That I loved them. She was still crying. She wanted me to promise things... impossible things. That nothing bad would ever happen to them. That she would never be sad. That she would never be alone.” She turned her eyes toward the window. The city slid by, and the lights reflected on the glass like cracks. “I left her crying,” she said. “I left her like that.” Then she added, after a second, “When he came to pick up the children, he saw the boarding pass on the dresser. He understood that I was leaving to come and see you.” Jacques wanted to take her hand. He first rested his fingers on the fabric of her coat, hesitated, then finally laid his hand on hers, gently, without squeezing. They drove on for a while longer. The driver kept his eyes on the road, indifferent. That protected them. When they reached the building, Jacques paid, took the suitcase, and they went upstairs. In the flat, warmth enveloped them. The sea beyond the windows was black. Stéphanie took off her shoes slowly. Jacques offered her tea. Stéphanie sat on the edge of the sofa. She looked at the living room, the large bay windows, the view, that silent luxury, and her face tightened for a second with exhaustion. She passed a hand quickly over her eyes. Jacques sat down beside her without touching her. He waited. Stéphanie finally turned her head toward him. “I’m sorry,” she said. “I can’t stop seeing the despair on my daughter Juliette’s face.” Jacques shook his head. This weekend would not be the one he had imagined. ## 4. The Shockwave Stéphanie fell asleep early that night. Jacques opened an Omnipollo beer, then a second, and finally a third, without finding in it the taste of rest. Around midnight he went to join her in the bedroom and slipped in beside her. On Saturday they went out late. Jacques suggested a walk in Djurgården. “Come on. It’ll take your mind off things.” Stéphanie’s eyes, usually so quick to laughter, opened wide all at once with the clear joy of a child being handed a sweet. Jacques felt his heart grow warm. They walked a long time without purpose through the royal park. At times Stéphanie stopped facing the sea to catch her breath. Jacques, beside her, accepted that rhythm. They went into a little café. Stéphanie smiled, then her face closed again, almost imperceptibly, with the tiniest crease at the corner of her eye. She had received a message. She looked at the screen without opening it at once, then placed the phone face down on the table. “Is it your daughter?” Jacques asked. Stéphanie nodded. “She’s better,” she said, unconvincingly. “She’s stopped crying. But she asked me if I was coming back. Twice.” She had spoken the words “coming back” with a cold weariness. Jacques felt powerless, the way one feels powerless in front of a sick child. He thought of the father’s remark, of the vile character of that thrust. He said nothing. He knew that to name it would be to give it more room. They went on walking. Parents were pulling sleds. The children laughed, sending sharp cries into the white air. Stéphanie looked at them, and Jacques had the impression she was looking at something else as well, some fulfilled family life she might have had, or had believed she possessed. On the way home they did a little shopping at the supermarket without thinking about it. Pasta, salmon, dark bread, fruit. In the flat Jacques set the bags down on the worktop, switched on a lamp, and the room filled with domestic warmth. Stéphanie took off her sweater and washed her hands for a long time. Jacques watched her without interrupting. She sat down on the sofa with her legs tucked beneath her, the phone on her knees. She sent a message, received another, replied again. Her concentration had something both moving and cruel about it. She had to remain present here and over there, in two rooms separated by a sea and by one man’s bad faith. When she finally put the phone down, it was late. She closed her eyes for a moment. “I’m sorry,” she said. “I’m useless.”
