Fragile Grace 1. Prologue: The Handkerchief Jacques had been walking for twenty minutes through the streets of Stockholm when he realized he was lost. The streets of Södermalm looked alike, the facades remained mute, while a fine snow fell without a sound. He checked the address on his phone one last time. Nothing indicated the entrance. He hesitated, retraced his steps, and finally found a door. The air inside was warm. The heat carried perfumes, alcohol, the smell of skin. The music throbbed, not very loudly, but with an obstinate regularity, like a heart trying to imitate life. Jacques took off his coat and handed it to a woman who smiled back at him. He had no social appetite, but forced himself to cross the first room. Women and men moved about slowly. He recognized the dim light, the laughter, the bursts of conversation, the glances offered and withdrawn, and that strange courtesy of desire when it becomes collective. Jacques spotted the bar, then, at the back, a second room. He exchanged a few words in Swedish and took the flute of champagne that was handed to him. He settled near a loudspeaker, his back to the wall. He observed. He thought of London, of those nights when he had convinced himself he was not looking for vice. In London, the crowd was noisier, more festive, more hurried. Here, everything seemed restrained. The city outside seemed still to be watching bodies through the walls. He wandered, moved into another room, then came back. He already felt tired. The week had seemed empty to him, as it often had since his arrival. He had signed a one-year contract. He worked for an investment bank. He passed through days from which he retained nothing. He was paid to wait. That was when he saw them. They occupied the center of a larger room, and the space seemed to arrange itself around them. A young man, perhaps in his twenties, possessed an insolent, almost mythological beauty. A tall blonde Swedish woman had a diaphanous delicacy. They were making love without haste. There was in their movements a quiet necessity. Around them, people watched the scene, some with a gentle avidity, others with an almost scientific curiosity. The young man, for his part, saw nothing. He was not performing. He was there, intent, fervent. For him, the world had narrowed to a patch of skin, a breath, a simple determination to live. The young woman unsettled Jacques by her face. That face spoke of trust, surrender, something that resembled love. In a place like that, such truth seemed almost indecent. They went on. Time loosened. They appeared to him like beings before the Fall, naked but without shame, strangers to the world around them. In that club saturated with eyes, they possessed, by some miracle, a scandalous innocence. Jacques felt a cold jealousy rising within him before the obvious bond that united them, that peace at the very heart of flesh. He was not merely outside. He was already after it all, after the Fall, after the expulsion, condemned to desire without ever believing. What struck him was the sense of something divine in the secret accord between those two beings. He watched their gestures the way one watches, from the snow, a lit window, and he knew himself to be in exile. Then the young man withdrew, and their embrace came apart. Afterward, she took a handkerchief with ceremonial gentleness. First she wiped the sweat-drenched forehead of her lover, performing that simple, intimate, domestic gesture, a gesture of home in the middle of the spectacle. Then she knelt and wiped what had fallen onto the floor, without disgust, without haste, with calm attention. Cleaning became a way of loving. And only afterward, with the same cloth she folded back over itself, she wiped her own sex, gleaming and wet. Jacques remained motionless. The order of it struck him. He saw in it a tiny household liturgy. He recognized in it a way of arranging the world around another person, of protecting him, of taking care of him even down to his traces. The music went on. Conversations resumed around them. He swallowed the rest of his champagne. The liquid seemed lukewarm. He set the glass down. Then he turned around, retrieved his coat, thanked someone mechanically, and went out. Outside, the snow had begun again. A white dust settled patiently onto the pavements, the facades, the motionless cars. The cold bit him. The warmth of the club vanished at once. His footsteps made a dull sound in the snow. He took out his phone. The screen lit his fingers. For a moment he stood looking at that small, ridiculous light in the middle of the black city. 2. Consensus On Monday morning Jacques woke at seven. He drank three cups of coffee while looking at the sea from the living room of the sumptuous three-room apartment he had rented in Östermalm, facing Nybroviken. The windows, too large, gave the water the dignity of a witness. He thought about the week ahead. The phone vibrated on the coffee table, screen against the wood, like a small impatient animal. Jacques turned it over. Stéphanie had written early, from London. “Did you do anything this weekend? Did you go out?” He looked at the question for a moment, so simple in itself. He thought of the club, of that handkerchief which had awakened in him the jealousy of a penitent. He felt the absurd urge to tell her everything, to unburden himself, to purify himself. He typed, “I went...” Then he deleted it. He typed again, “Not much.” He hesitated once more, added a sentence, deleted that too. In the end he sent: “I took it easy. I miss you.” When the message went, his shoulders dropped. He put the phone away. He told himself he would explain later. He already knew he would explain nothing. He put on his long coat, his gloves, his wool hat, and went out. He strode down Strandvägen, caught in the glacial cold of February. He hurried toward his office, right in the center of the capital. Since his arrival at the Swedish investment bank where he had signed on for a year, people had been asking his opinion on everything, then thanking him with exaggerated solemnity. No decision seemed complete until it had been “validated” by an Anglo-Saxon consultant paid at a princely rate. Most of them worked no more than two hours a day, with the gravity of priests serving a godless cult. Jacques put down his coat, fetched another coffee, opened his computer. In his inbox three invitations to “catch-ups” had piled up overnight. He accepted them without reading, by reflex. In his mind he arranged the chairs for the weekly meeting. He already knew half the team would be missing. The English and the Germans arrived on Mondays as one joins a distant war, in waves, from London and Frankfurt, sometime between eleven and four. The last, who came from Luxembourg, indulged, or rather had indulged for him, the luxury of beginning his week at five in the evening, invoking the lack of a direct flight. It sometimes happened that Jacques crossed paths with him at the entrance while, in the elevator, Swedes were already descending homeward with the peaceful look of those who had finished their day. The head of department had decided the meeting would remain at nine. She had considered, briefly, organizing one every hour of the day, then abandoned the idea. At nine Jacques joined his Swedish colleagues in the meeting room. They were talking about their weekend cross-country skiing with the family, the children bundled in snowsuits, dragged in little sleds like projects one hauls along, smiling, to the point of exhaustion. The head of the department was called Alma. On the first day Jacques had thought she must once have been very beautiful. After thirty-four years in banking, she ran research and IT development with the dry energy of survivors, that way of staying upright in the middle of ruins. She began, as always, with a sobriety meant to reassure. “We need a consensus.” In that bank, consensus had the status of a mythological creature. Everybody spoke of it, nobody had ever seen it, and as soon as one approached its natural habitat it disappeared into the undergrowth of “dependencies” and “budget not validated.” Slides were shown. Someone asked what “consensus” meant exactly in the present context. Someone else remarked that the budget had not been validated. A third started speaking about dependencies with the same gravity as an addiction specialist. Voices overlapped, then died away, then returned, each defending its little patch of territory with cutting politeness. Jacques watched their hands: the ones fiddling with a pen, the ones smoothing the corner of a sheet of paper, the ones typing a message under the table. Consensus did not occur. It never occurred. Jacques was already no longer listening. The evening two days earlier came back to him in fragments: skin, muted light, and above all that handkerchief. He preferred not to share his thoughts with the team. Alma would manage very well without his ideas, he thought. Jacques remembered that the colleague sitting beside him had nearly strangled another one the previous week, and so he nodded prudently at every sentence he uttered. Independent consultants like him, as a bonus, attracted a murkier, less confessable hostility, as though their mere presence silently reminded everyone of the failure to manage without them. An hour later, everyone left the meeting hating a little more those with whom they had failed to agree. Alma, for her part, retained that satisfying impression of having moved things forward, which is to say of having shifted, without solving them, the same problems from one week to the next. At eleven in the morning the Swedish colleagues went downstairs in clusters for lunch in the basement. Jacques had declined the invitation on his first day; it was never renewed. The office emptied. A peace settled in. Jacques sat down on a sofa, took out his iPhone 7, and gave himself over to his favorite pastime: sliding his index finger from left to right as quickly as possible. Now and then a woman’s face appeared: smiling promise, first name, quotation. Jacques pasted the same message every time, with the quiet efficiency of a diligent employee, the way one fills out forms in the hope that one day the French administration might reply. After twenty-five minutes, the profiles ran dry. The screen flickered, then offered up the sidereal void. There were no more candidates available for the moment. He placed the phone face down on the sofa. He went out for a walk as far as Kungsträdgården. Outside, the air cut. The black branches held themselves against the pale sky. He returned an hour later, sat down at his workstation, and resumed work with impeccable seriousness. It was two in the afternoon; most of his English colleagues had just settled at their desks. Doctor Fynn came over to him. “How are you, Jacques? Did you stay in Stockholm over the weekend? What did you do?” Jacques nodded. “Not much,” he answered vaguely. “My partner, Stéphanie, is divorced. She had to look after her children. She stayed in London, as often happens. She didn’t join me.” “How did the meeting go this morning? Did you speak to Alma about my new model?” “No,” Jacques replied. He had forgotten. Fynn was a renowned mathematician, one of those who had managed to turn solid academic aptitude into a lucrative career. He had made a fortune by implementing statistical trading algorithms inside a New York hedge fund. Back in London, he had treated himself to a splendid Edwardian house in Hammersmith, facing the Thames, under the continuous rumble of planes coming in to land at Heathrow. When he spoke, he twisted his tie with methodical nervousness until it had worn thin. He wore trousers too short for him, exposing scuffed Tod’s shoes unsuited to the snow in Stockholm; every time he stepped outside, he wrapped them in little transparent plastic overshoes, which amused Jacques greatly. His work consisted in putting a price on the future. He called it a model. Jacques watched him speak and sometimes wondered whether it was not above all a polite way of organizing fear: fear of the unforeseen movement, of the day the market remembers that it does not obey equations. Fynn assumed uncertainty followed a law. For a long time Jacques had believed in the usefulness of his profession. He was no longer so sure. In the physical world, models describe an indifferent reality, one that repeats itself. Here, Fynn was trying to capture unstable quantities contaminated by man, by his panics, his greed, and his tricks. Models possessed the elegance of coherent lies. Jacques remembered London, eight years earlier. He had worked on assets linked to credit risk, the infamous CDOs that had despoiled widow, orphan, retiree, and invalid with mathematical elegance. He had been astonished to see the systems developed by the bank’s learned researchers produce such extravagant valuations. Behind the screens there was a false serenity. One day he had taken the lift to speak to research about it. “And who are you? Ah, the frog downstairs? Next time make an appointment. Are you a PhD? Don’t try to understand. Don’t even try.” Jacques had gone back down to the basement. He had tried to understand all the same, a little, tapping away on his first-generation iPhone. Copula, Google had answered him affably. He went on coding. Six months later he went upstairs to present his system to the credit desk. They made him wait. He asked where his users were. No one seemed to know. He insisted, politely. Then he came upon an obese man in his thirties, a cardboard box in one hand, a beer in the other. His shirt pulled across his stomach. “Do you know where the credit desk sits?” Jacques had inquired. “You’re late, mate. I’m the last one. I’ve just been fired.” He had said it with a smile, without bitterness, with that thoroughly British good nature. He shrugged, half mocking, and added, “Pack up your software. Grab a box. There are some over there.” And Jacques had seen himself for a moment standing there with his computer, his seriousness, and his useless equations, in the middle of a collapse that people referred to simply as “restructuring.” 3. Arrivals Hall Three days later, on Thursday evening, Jacques left the office at four. He picked up his computer, buttoned his coat, crossed the open-plan office with that mixture of discretion and haste which, in open-plan offices, arouses suspicion. In the lift he felt cheerful at the thought of being reunited with Stéphanie for a few days. Her children were with their father until Monday. Outside, night had already fallen. He hailed a Tesla taxi. The cabin smelled of new leather and cutting-edge technology. The driver asked no questions. Jacques merely said: Arlanda. They crossed Vasastan. Then the center receded. The facades lowered, the lights grew sparse, and the road unrolled ahead. Jacques took out his iPhone 7, then put it away again. He busied himself with absurd details: what he would say when he saw her, how he would place his hand on her suitcase, whether he should kiss her at once or wait. He had the same nervousness as before an important meeting, that habit of preparing sentences so as not to be caught off guard by emotion. He thought of London. Of last summer. London, in summer, is unlike itself. The city ceases for a moment to be a machine and becomes a setting again. People linger, pubs overflow, the parks turn into makeshift beaches. Even the light there seems softer, more indulgent. The memory of Stéphanie rose within him, tender and precise. Jacques had loved her passionately. Her scent, intoxicating and sensual, and that face marked by a silent kindness had seized him from the first encounter. She was raising two children alone in London, in a small flat overlooking Cleveland Square, with its carefully tended garden in the center, the sort one finds in the wealthy districts of west London. She had separated from her husband six months before meeting Jacques. He saw again their first night together. She had invited him to her place one summer evening, windows open over the little square garden, and they had watched Leviathan, that Russian film. He could see the sea again: a cold shore, rusted sheets of metal, and on the beach the white carcass of a whale. The story amounted to very little: a house too well situated, a coveted plot of land, and a man being told that what was his would be taken from him, in that tone some French notaries affect. They speak as though the state belonged to them, and burden the citizen with their indignity. Stéphanie had murmured, almost without moving, “They’re going to crush him.” Jacques had nodded. The court droned out its decision, the police smiled, friends drank, and the Church stood there, motionless. Then their bodies had begun to seek each other, and the film had gone on behind them. They had found each other at an age when one no longer searches, because one knows oneself well enough. Jacques was thirty-eight. She was forty. Beside her, he had known a peace, a fullness, of which he felt himself incapable. He knew she had left her husband a year earlier. He knew about the children, the logistics, the solitude. He knew too, through hints and silences, that the separation had been a detonation in Stéphanie’s life and in the children’s. But he did not know why. Stéphanie had never wanted to tell him. He had not pressed her, waiting for her to confide in him. The taxi was approaching Arlanda. The signs lined up, the exits followed one another. Jacques felt his stomach tighten. He was going to see her again, and the thought made him light. He would breathe in her suave scent when she came to nestle against him. In the arrivals hall, the warmth of the airport had something inhuman about it. Travelers slid past with rolling suitcases and tired eyes. Jacques took up position near the automatic doors. He waited. When she appeared, he did not recognize her at first. The journey, the fatigue, everything had made her thinner, more taut. She held her phone in one hand, her suitcase in the other. She searched for Jacques with her eyes, and when she saw him, something relaxed in her face, only for a second, then that reserve he had first noticed returned. They moved toward each other. He was about to say something awkward, one word too many, a needless joke. He opened his mouth, then fell silent. Stéphanie got there first. She laid a hand on his sleeve, a brief, sure, verifying touch, to make certain he was real. “I’m here,” she said. Jacques embraced her for a long time. He took her hand and they walked toward the exit. In the taxi on the way back they spoke about the weekend’s plans. Jacques proposed going skiing at Hellasgården. Stockholm flowed past, clean and black. Jacques looked at Stéphanie. She was staring at the road, her hands resting motionless on her bag. He felt the urge rise in him to touch her, to undo her, to bring her back to the body. Stéphanie breathed in, then out slowly. Jacques thought she was going to speak of her week. Instead she said, “I had my daughter on the phone. Just before boarding. She was crying.” Jacques moved closer to her. “What’s wrong?” Stéphanie hesitated for a second. Then she spoke in a controlled voice, without pathos, which made it harsher. “Their father told them... He told them, ‘Do you know where your mother is? She’s in Sweden this weekend. Anything could happen to you.’” She paused. “She’s seven. “She heard that and imagined... she imagined I was abandoning her, her and her little brother. That something might happen to them and I would not be there. She cried as though someone were pushing her into a hole.” Jacques felt a dry anger rising in him. He thought of the simple cruelty of adults, of the way children are used as weapons because it is more effective than an argument. He thought of what it must have cost Stéphanie to board that flight and come to join him. Stéphanie went on, more softly: “I kept repeating that I was coming back. That I wasn’t leaving them. That I loved them. She was still crying. She wanted me to promise things... impossible things. That nothing bad would ever happen to them. That she would never be sad. That she would never be alone.” She turned her eyes toward the window. The city slid by, and the lights reflected on the glass like cracks. “I left her crying,” she said. “I left her like that.” Then she added, after a second, “When he came to pick up the children, he saw the boarding pass on the dresser. He understood that I was leaving to come and see you.” Jacques wanted to take her hand. He first rested his fingers on the fabric of her coat, hesitated, then finally laid his hand on hers, gently, without squeezing. They drove on for a while longer. The driver kept his eyes on the road, indifferent. That protected them. When they reached the building, Jacques paid, took the suitcase, and they went upstairs. In the flat, warmth enveloped them. The sea beyond the windows was black. Stéphanie took off her shoes slowly. Jacques offered her tea. Stéphanie sat on the edge of the sofa. She looked at the living room, the large bay windows, the view, that silent luxury, and her face tightened for a second with exhaustion. She passed a hand quickly over her eyes. Jacques sat down beside her without touching her. He waited. Stéphanie finally turned her head toward him. “I’m sorry,” she said. “I can’t stop seeing the despair on my daughter Juliette’s face.” Jacques shook his head. This weekend would not be the one he had imagined. 4. The Shockwave Stéphanie fell asleep early that night. Jacques opened an Omnipollo beer, then a second, and finally a third, without finding in it the taste of rest. Around midnight he went to join her in the bedroom and slipped in beside her. On Saturday they went out late. Jacques suggested a walk in Djurgården. “Come on. It’ll take your mind off things.” Stéphanie’s eyes, usually so quick to laughter, opened wide all at once with the clear joy of a child being handed a sweet. Jacques felt his heart grow warm. They walked a long time without purpose through the royal park. At times Stéphanie stopped facing the sea to catch her breath. Jacques, beside her, accepted that rhythm. They went into a little café. Stéphanie smiled, then her face closed again, almost imperceptibly, with the tiniest crease at the corner of her eye. She had received a message. She looked at the screen without opening it at once, then placed the phone face down on the table. “Is it your daughter?” Jacques asked. Stéphanie nodded. “She’s better,” she said, unconvincingly. “She’s stopped crying. But she asked me if I was coming back. Twice.” She had spoken the words “coming back” with a cold weariness. Jacques felt powerless, the way one feels powerless in front of a sick child. He thought of the father’s remark, of the vile character of that thrust. He said nothing. He knew that to name it would be to give it more room. They went on walking. Parents were pulling sleds. The children laughed, sending sharp cries into the white air. Stéphanie looked at them, and Jacques had the impression she was looking at something else as well, some fulfilled family life she might have had, or had believed she possessed. On the way home they did a little shopping at the supermarket without thinking about it. Pasta, salmon, dark bread, fruit. In the flat Jacques set the bags down on the worktop, switched on a lamp, and the room filled with domestic warmth. Stéphanie took off her sweater and washed her hands for a long time. Jacques watched her without interrupting. She sat down on the sofa with her legs tucked beneath her, the phone on her knees. She sent a message, received another, replied again. Her concentration had something both moving and cruel about it. She had to remain present here and over there, in two rooms separated by a sea and by one man’s bad faith. When she finally put the phone down, it was late. She closed her eyes for a moment. “I’m sorry,” she said. “I’m useless.” 5. The Solstice In June, in Stockholm, night no longer really fell. It withdrew, dissolved, and left behind a dawn-paleness that came in through the bay windows, laying a medical clarity over the furniture. Facing Nybroviken, the sea had lost the blackness of February. It was moving at last, scratched by quick reflections. Gulls cried above the boats, demanding their share of the season. Four months earlier, Jacques had thought he had found a balance. Then balance had revealed itself to be a posture. Stéphanie and he had drifted apart without any crash. There had been no scene, not even any final words. She came to Sweden less often; Jacques’s trips to London had grown rarer. Jacques noticed that the custody calendar she used to share with him was no longer updated, which hurt him deeply. The lie in which she had lived for so long during her marriage had made her resistant to any deeper commitment. Her children had entered their relationship, inevitably. Her former life had weakened their bond. Jacques too had withdrawn when passion lost some of its force. The faults were shared. With spring, Stockholm widened. Temptations grew more violent as nature, and people, came back to themselves. Terraces spread across the pavements; one could hear glasses clinking late into the night and laughter without conclusion. The parks filled with bodies stretched out in the grass; skin offered itself to the rare sun. In Hagaparken, near Brunnsviken, groups sprawled out barefoot, shoulders bare, music turned low. A little farther on, the forest began. One could take the metro, walk ten minutes, and find oneself at the edge of a still lake ringed with pines. The air there smelled of resin and restored one’s confidence in life. On weekends, posters announced open-air concerts, temporary stages along the quays, orchestras in parks. The city needed noise in order to forget that it had spent nine months keeping quiet. Even people seemed to speak more loudly, as though winter silence had left behind the fear of vanishing. Jacques was still working. Or rather, he still went to the office. The bank had not changed. He passed through empty days in which his role had been reduced to that of a body sitting in a chair. Summer made that emptiness even more humiliating, because all the Swedes, meanwhile, were enjoying two months of holiday. He felt exhausted by the absence of effort. There was, however, one notable exception, one moment of truth, one only, each month, that jolted him out of his existential torpor. Toward the end of the month, his mind woke all at once. His attention fixed on numbers with a mystical intensity. He recovered an energy he had not possessed in the previous weeks, and devoted it to this administrative ritual: manufacturing proof that he had existed. On this 21st of June, he opened his file, the same one he always reused for the accounts of the company he had founded in London six years earlier, LatentEdge Capital. He counted the days on which he had given body and soul. Twenty-one. He typed: “Daily rate: £1,200 per day.” He did the calculation slowly, and checked it several times, afraid of getting it wrong: 21 × £1,200. He wrote: £25,200. The Excel sheet automatically added VAT. The total rose to £30,240 for the month of June. He added the rent on his flat, which he had negotiated separately: SEK 53,000, or £4,758. The same spreadsheet performed the sum: £34,998. The figure gave him a brief, mechanical satisfaction. It had the purity of an exact result. A month had just taken shape. It had been justified. Then the emptiness returned to its place. That evening he came home very late. It was not dark. Stockholm refused to offer him that relief. The light persisted, stubborn, stuck to the facades, the pavements, the panes of glass. It had the obstinacy of an inspection. In the flat, he poured himself a glass of water, sat down, and opened the dating app. He liked the repetitive gesture. What he liked even more was the absence of immediate consequence. One could desire without committing, make contact without compromising oneself, disappear without explanation. It was a poor freedom, but it was freedom. He scrolled through the profiles. One face stopped his finger. Tanned face, long hair; a woman wore a broad smile that revealed very white teeth. On a tropical beach, her green and yellow bikini left no room for ambiguity. Manuela. Bio: “Brazilian. Researcher at Karolinska. Coffee, museums, long walks. Relationship goal: still figuring it out.” He swiped right. Match. She answered quickly, in very good English: “Coffee this week?” He said yes, too quickly. They fixed on Thursday, six in the evening, near Odenplan, in a café where one could sit outside. 6. Locks On Thursday, at six in the evening, in June, Stockholm still looked as if it were the middle of the afternoon. The city had bare skin. The light refused to leave bodies in peace. Time no longer counted. People left work without haste. Bicycles skimmed past the tables. A pram creaked over a slab of stone. Jacques arrived early. He sat at a table and watched. He watched a great deal now. He had picked up the habit, perhaps to compensate for the fact that he barely did anything anymore. Watching gave the illusion of participation. Manuela arrived on time. She was smaller than he had imagined. She carried a bag that was too heavy, and thick glasses. She smiled briefly. “Jacques?” “Manuela.” She sat down, placed her bag on the ground, and sighed. “I think I’m carrying my whole life in there.” He smiled. She had an accent, not a strong one, but there was a particular music in certain consonants. She ordered a glass of white wine. Jacques took a Falcon. The waitress left. Silence settled between them, not awkward, more cautious. They first spoke about the city, the light, and the way summer in Sweden seemed like a form of compensation. “People imagine summer here is gentle,” she said. “I find it tyrannical. “You never know when to stop. You never know when to sleep.” Jacques nodded. He told her he worked in a bank. He saw in her eyes a weary amusement. “Finance?” “Yes. Well... I mostly perform the act of being there.” She laughed softly. “At Karolinska it’s the same. We do research, and spend half our time waiting.” They left the café and walked. They went up toward Hagaparken. The water of Brunnsviken gleamed softly. People were swimming. Others lay motionless in the grass; they were practicing being happy. Farther on, the forest began, dense and silent. Manuela breathed more deeply. “This is what I like here. You can be in the city, and suddenly you have nature. In Brazil, in a big city like São Paulo, nature is far away. Here, it’s beside you.” “Yes,” said Jacques. “I often come and run in this park.” She asked him what exactly he did. He answered with an honesty that surprised even him. “I bill for my time.” “In order to waste it,” he added at once. “How much?” He hesitated, then said: “Twelve hundred pounds a day.” She stopped short at the edge of a path. He had to stop too. She looked at him, without anger, without admiration. Only lucidly. “That’s a lot.” “Yes. And the worst part is that I almost have nothing to do.” He expected a retort. She gave none. She resumed walking, more slowly. He had just said something exact, and it had lightened him. They went on. A group was playing music farther away. There was a burst of applause. In June, the city had the air of a permanent festival. Manuela resumed, naturally, speaking of her work. She explained without trying to seduce. She spoke because it was her material. “I work on reproductive endocrinology. Hormones, feedback loops. The way the body makes decisions without consulting us.” A bicycle passed too close, its bell snapping. Manuela stepped aside, and for a moment her shoulder brushed Jacques’s. He felt the warmth of her skin through the light fabric. Jacques smiled to hide his disquiet. “You chose a cheerful field.” She replied without laughing: “Not cheerful. Fundamental. People like to romanticize sex. In the lab, sex is mostly chemistry and timing.” They were walking by the water. Groups were laughing, cans were opening, a child cried out with joy in the distance. Jacques felt the conversation sliding toward a place he wanted to go, and feared to go. He asked: “People often say female pleasure isn’t necessary for reproduction. Is that true?” Manuela nodded, without triumph. She looked him straight in the eye. “In humans, strictly speaking, yes. Orgasm isn’t required. A woman can conceive without it.” Jacques waited. She added, with cold precision: “But people also say, ‘male orgasm is necessary,’ and that isn’t literally true either. What is necessary is ejaculation. The two usually go together, but not always. There are rare cases where ejaculation occurs without orgasm. And cases where orgasm occurs without ejaculation. Biology doesn’t care about pleasure. It wants sperm in the right place.” Jacques gave a brief laugh. Orgasm without ejaculation was more enviable than ejaculation without orgasm. “So the injustice is subtler than the formula. It isn’t ‘the man must enjoy and the woman need not.’ It’s more: the man must deliver something, and the woman must already be ready, whether she wants to be or not.” Manuela looked at him as though she recognized a thought she had seen in others before, but rarely heard put so clearly. “Yes. And that asymmetry spills beyond biology. Culture takes hold of it. “Sometimes it exaggerates it. Sometimes it punishes it.” They passed near a group playing music. A drum improvised from a bucket, a few bursts of applause. Manuela waited until they had moved away. “You were asking whether there are species where it’s different, right?” “Yes.” Her face lit up with a brief technical joy. “Yes. Induced ovulators. Rabbits, cats, ferrets. In those species, the female doesn’t ovulate on a regular cycle as we do. “Mating triggers a neuroendocrine reflex, and ovulation follows.” Jacques pictured it, and it seemed to him more logical, in a brutal way. “A door that only opens if someone knocks.” “Exactly. By late afternoon Jacques felt a form of discouragement before the complexity of the task set before him. Around nine Marc came up to him with that quick smile men wear when they scent a kinship. “You’re still here? Let’s get out. Want a drink?” Jacques accepted at once. He had no desire to return alone to his flat, nor to dine alone in a food court. The city exerted a particular pressure on solitary men. The air remained thick, heavy, crossed by smells of frying oil, perfume and spilled beer. On the opposite bank the towers of the financial district lit up one by one with a functional majesty. At first I was meant to stay six months. Then they extended. Then they panicked. So they extended again. It’s always the same in these programmes. He knew that tone. He had heard it before in the mouths of certain expatriates, often after two beers and three months of residence. Your mistake is thinking it exhausts everything.” Marc set down his glass. “A very fine formula. But human history mostly shows the opposite. Jacques went on. “Someone still has to save something. Otherwise nothing remains but transactions.” For the first time Marc truly hesitated. It was brief, but Jacques felt it. Jacques looked at Marc, his thin face, his quickness, his still youthful hardness. There was in him something both typical and singular: an intelligent, profitable, mobile man already worn out by a world that had taught him to think everything in terms of return, including his freedom. In others, cynicism remained a complaint. In him, it had disciplined itself until it became method. When they parted a little later, Jacques watched him move away along the quay, a dry, quick, nervous silhouette. Marc walked with the haste of men who want to leave the world while continuing to extract income from it. He gave the impression of fleeing less a place than a condition. Jacques climbed slowly back toward the towers. The city shone around him. He told himself that Singapore attracted that kind of man for a simple reason: it resembled them. It offered each the possibility of verifying his own theory of the world. It had no scruples, only objectives. It promised to anyone who accepted its logic a cleaner, more efficient, lighter existence. In exchange it seemed to require that one abandon without noise everything resistant to calculation. On returning to his apartment he thought again of the conversation. Marc had displeased him in places, amused him in others, at times even impressed him despite himself. There was in his dry theories something deeply contemporary. He was not only a cynic. He was a man who wanted to convert every wound into system, every humiliation into calculation, every dependence into an exit strategy. In the lift, the mirror returned to him his tired face. He had the sudden obscure sensation of having encountered less an individual than a possibility of himself, someone who had already transformed his weariness into method. Tambun Selatan The months slipped by in Singapore. There was not really any season in the tropics. Jacques watched time pass without being able to anchor it to anything. The light changed little, the rains returned with sovereign regularity, and the weeks ended by resembling one another in a moist softness where everything seemed suspended. He saw Dewi again between each of her stopovers. At every visit she brought him dates from Doha, chocolates from Dubai, sometimes vodka bought in the duty-free zone. She had the diligent generosity of people who have known lack and who, as soon as they earn a little money, feel the need to redistribute its signs. Their affair had settled into tenderness and complicity. She appeared, disappeared, flew back to Doha, London or Seoul, then returned with her immense smile and her stopover stories. One day she suggested they spend a few days visiting her family in the outer eastern suburbs of Jakarta, in Tambun Selatan. They left the congested main roads, went past rows of low shops, workshops, garages, petrol stations, then entered a poorer district crossed by cement alleys, narrow ditches and hanging wires. As they advanced, nothing remained but an accumulation of low houses, corrugated metal, washing and dust. Dewi came from there. Her family lived in a kampung in Bekasi. She had often spoken to him of that house. She had the reserve of people from modest origins. Jacques had expected something more miserable. The house stood at the end of an alley so narrow that two motorbikes could barely pass one another. It was wedged between two neighbouring constructions. Dewi had had it built little by little with the salary Qatar Airways paid her. It was no longer the precarious shack of her childhood. The small house was single-storey, solid, built in stages as money became available. First one room and a decent roof. Then a second bedroom. Later still, cheap tiling, a sheet-metal awning to protect the entrance, and at the back a masoned space where one could cook cleanly. The plot was narrow. The house must have measured six by seven metres. A motorbike was parked in front of the door under the metal awning. Laundry dried on lines stretched between a post and the shared wall. Inside, the layout remained simple. The front room served at once as living room, dining room and common room for the whole family. A sofa covered in brown fabric ran along the wall. A television sat on a plywood cabinet. An oscillating fan stirred the warm air with a continuous buzzing. Behind that first room a very short corridor led to two small bedrooms. In one slept Dewi’s parents. In the other, her younger brothers and sister, crowded on thin mattresses folded away in the morning to clear a little space. At the back was the kitchen, narrow but solidly built, with a sink, a gas bottle, a few shelves, hanging saucepans. The washroom and toilets were behind, under a more rudimentary extension roofed with corrugated sheets. There was a bucket there, a basin, a tired showerhead. Every metre seemed to have been conquered. Every improvement must have awaited a transfer, a bonus, an extra rotation. Jacques looked at the walls, the floor, the electrical outlets, the uneven tiles, and understood what a salary paid in foreign currency could represent in such a place. It was a slow, fragmentary, stubborn material victory, real enough all the same to change the life of an entire family. The house remained open for much of the day. Neighbours stuck their heads in, called from one doorway to another, borrowed a tool, returned a dish, commented on the rain, prices, traffic jams, a child’s health. Privacy there was limited, the noise continuous, the heat persistent. Mutual aid circulated with an obviousness rich districts have long since lost. Jacques was struck less by the poverty than by that density of life. Dewi spoke little when she was there. She translated a few sentences, smiled, answered her mother, gently scolded her youngest brother, discreetly gave money to her father. She no longer quite belonged in that house, and yet remained its economic centre. When they left, they crossed the alley again in the dirty light of the afternoon. Dewi walked a little ahead of him. Children were playing near the ditch, women chatting under an awning, a call to prayer rising from a nearby mosque, distorted by bad loudspeakers. Jacques turned one last time toward the small faded-colour house. Dewi had not only paid for walls, a roof and tiling. She had bought her family a little stability, a little dignity, and that precious conviction that existence, through work, can finally manage to stand upright. In the evening they returned to Singapore. He saw Dewi as the point of junction between two worlds whose gap now suddenly seemed immense. 16. Learning His mission was dragging on. The project was bogging down with that viscous slowness peculiar to large organisations. Jacques now divided his time between the financial district at Marina Bay and Changi Business Park. Twice a day, a small shuttle chartered by the bank carried him from one end of the island to the other. He found in it a respite. He had made it his principal source of satisfaction. His manager, a heavy, suspicious and petty man, had taken to standing behind him to observe his screen and his gestures with continuous vigilance. Departure He returned to Paterson Hill a little earlier than usual. The security guard in the lobby gave him the same discreet greeting as on other evenings. He put his bag on the floor, took off his shoes, and remained standing in the middle of the sitting room. The large bay windows looked out onto towers and black vegetation. Below, a few headlights slid slowly between the palms. He opened the refrigerator. Jacques ended up asking: “How far along?” “I don’t know exactly. Five weeks. ” “Have you seen anyone?” “I called my medical insurer during the day. I have an appointment next Friday.” She was saying all this simply, with her usual efficiency. “It happened when you came back from Zagreb, that Sunday, remember?” she said, smiling. Jacques remembered it very well. Lyubov had gone to Moscow to see her parents for about ten days. The complications of travelling to Russia had discouraged him from accompanying her. He had stayed in London to work, then spent the end of the week in Zagreb. He had lodged there in a flat in the old town, with high ceilings, large windows, and a view over a very steep cobbled street. Before returning, Lyubov had written to him, in her tender seriousness, to say that her ovulation would coincide exactly with the moment when they were reunited. The message had made him smile. He had promised himself to come back less late. Naturally, he had arrived exhausted and frozen. They had made love quickly, then fallen asleep. Lyubov had wanted a child for longer than he had. She had spoken of it to him often, with a calm gravity. She had read a great deal, knew the figures, the deadlines, the probabilities of conceiving at the beginning of one’s forties. They had stopped all contraception a few months earlier, during a trip to Iceland. Jacques would not have been able to say at what exact moment his reluctance had fallen away. Perhaps the solitude of that island, its depopulated and austere landscapes, had finally worn down his last resistance. Existence had appeared to him as an obstinate continuation. All the restaurants were already closed when Jacques had parked his Land Rover Defender in front of the remote cottage they had booked near Kalfafellsstadur. They had spent the afternoon around the glacier of Svinafellsjokull. The ice came down between the dark slopes in broad frozen folds, streaked with ash, blue in places with a dense blue, like a light trapped beneath the surface. They had left too late to have dinner. That evening they had eaten the sandwiches they had left, then made love with a young, imprudent fervour, like two beings who, for the first time, were consenting without reserve to what was to come. “I hadn’t expected to be pregnant so quickly,” she said. “Nor had I.” He was listening to her. He wanted to stand up, to go to her, to take her in his arms. Yet he remained seated, held back by something grave. He felt within himself a deep displacement. His life had just lost, in a single sentence, part of its reversible character. Until then, almost everything could still be undone, modified, left behind. A city, a mission, a woman sometimes, a habit, a future. Even love, so long as no child had passed through it, kept a secret exit door. Now that door was closing without violence, but with certainty. He thought of the end of that silent freedom which had long served him as a spine, the possibility of disappearing and leaving the future in a deliberately provisional state. That world was withdrawing. “What are you thinking about?” she asked. “Too many things at once.” She waited, then at last laid her hand on his. The touch was cool. Jacques lowered his eyes to their joined fingers. “You don’t have to react at once,” she said. After a few seconds, Jacques finally stood up, walked around the table and knelt beside her. He laid his head against her belly. Through the fabric of her dress he felt only the warmth of her body and the simplicity of her presence. Lyubov passed her hand through his hair. “There’s nothing to hear yet,” she said. “I know. ” Yet he remained like that for a few moments. When he stood up again, her eyes were slightly bright. He did not know whether she was about to cry. But the tears did not come. Later, they sat on the sofa without turning on the television. Lyubov had curled up against him, her legs drawn under the blanket they had brought back from a trip to Scotland. He offered to make tea. She accepted. They spoke little. A few sentences about the coming appointment, about the weeks during which they would still have to keep quiet, about the way they would one day announce it to those they loved. Then, little by little, the words withdrew. At eleven o’clock, she said she was going to sleep. In bed, she fell asleep before he did, as always. Jacques lay on his back looking into the darkness. An invisible being had just entered the world, without knowledge, without language, without defence, and was already demanding from them an attention no method could take on in their place. He thought too, with a lucidity free of pathos, that he would never again be alone in quite the same way. Even if one day he lost his job, even if the world reorganised itself around machines, even if society as a whole slid toward a form of generalised assistance, there would now exist this happy and terrible debt: he could no longer live as though his presence in the world remained optional. He turned his head toward Lyubov asleep. In the dark, he could barely make out her profile on the pillow. He laid his hand on the sheet, level with her belly, not touching her at first, then very lightly.
