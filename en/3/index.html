Chapter 2 The Arrivals Hall Three days later, on Thursday evening, Jacques left the office at four. He gathered up his laptop, buttoned his coat, and crossed the open-plan floor with that mixture of discretion and haste which, in open-plan offices, arouses suspicion. In the lift, he felt happy at the thought of seeing Stéphanie again for a few days. Her children were with their father until Monday. Outside, it was already dark. He hailed a Tesla taxi. The cabin smelled of new leather and advanced technology. The driver asked no questions. Jacques simply said: Arlanda. They crossed Vasastan. Then the centre fell away. The façades grew lower, the lights thinned out, and the road unfurled. Jacques took out his iPhone, then put it away again at once. He settled absurd details in his mind: what he would say when he saw her, how he would place his hand on her suitcase, whether he should kiss her straight away or wait. He felt the same nervousness as before an important meeting. He thought of London. In summer, people linger, pubs overflow, parks become improvised beaches. Stéphanie first came back to him through her sweet scent, then through that face of hers, which carried a silent kindness. She was raising two children alone in London, in a small flat overlooking Cleveland Square, with its garden in the centre, carefully maintained, the sort one finds in the prosperous districts of the west of the city. She had separated from her husband a year before meeting Jacques. He saw again their first night of love. She had invited him to her place one summer evening, the windows open onto the small square garden, and they had watched Leviathan, the Russian film. He remembered the sea: a cold shoreline, rusted sheet metal, and, on the beach, the white carcass of a whale. The story held together on very little: a coveted piece of land, and a man being told that what belonged to him was going to be taken away. Stéphanie had murmured, hardly moving: “They’re going to crush him.” Jacques had nodded. The court recited its decision, the police smiled, the friends drank, and the Church stood there, motionless. Then their bodies had sought each other out, and the film had gone on behind them. They had met too late for naïve beginnings, early enough still to believe that another life remained possible. Jacques was thirty-eight. She was forty. She had spoken to him at length about her children, about the difficult logistics of family life, and about what she called the lack of human warmth. He also knew, from allusions, from silences, that the separation had been a detonation in Stéphanie’s life and in the lives of the children. But he did not know why. Stéphanie had never wanted to tell him. He had not questioned her, waiting for her to open herself up. The taxi was approaching Arlanda. The signs lined up, the exits followed one another. Jacques felt his stomach tighten. He was going to see her again, and the thought made him light. In the arrivals hall, the airport heat was stifling. Travellers dragged wheeled suitcases, their eyes tired. Jacques stood near the automatic doors. He waited. When she appeared, he did not recognise her at once. She seemed thinner to him, more tense. She held her phone in one hand, her suitcase in the other. She searched for Jacques with her eyes, and when she saw him, something loosened on her face, for barely a second, before that restraint returned, the restraint he had first perceived in her. They came towards each other. He was about to say something clumsy, one word too many, some useless joke. He opened his mouth, then fell silent. Stéphanie spoke first. She placed a hand on his sleeve, a brief, sure, verifying contact. “I’m here,” she said. Jacques held her for a long time. He took her hand, and they walked towards the exit. In the taxi on the way back, they talked about the weekend. Jacques suggested going skiing at Hellasgården. Stockholm passed by outside. Jacques looked at Stéphanie. She was staring at the black road, her hands resting on her bag, motionless. He wanted to put his hand on her, to draw her against him. Stéphanie inhaled, then slowly exhaled. Jacques thought she was going to talk about her week. Instead, she said: “I had my daughter on the phone. Just before boarding.” Jacques moved closer to her. “What happened?” Stéphanie hesitated for a second. Then she spoke in a controlled voice, without pathos. “Their father told them… He said to them, ‘Do you know where your mother is? She’s in Sweden this weekend. Anything could happen to you.’” She paused. “She’s seven. She heard that and she imagined… she imagined I was abandoning them, her and her little brother. That someone could hurt them and I wouldn’t be there. She cried as if someone were pushing her into a hole.” Jacques felt a dull anger rise in him. Stéphanie went on, more quietly: “I kept telling her I was coming back. That I wasn’t leaving them. That I loved them. She kept crying. She wanted me to promise things… impossible things. That nothing would ever happen to them. That she would never be sad. That she would never be alone.” She turned her eyes towards the window. The city slid past, and on the glass the reflected lights looked like cracks. “I left her crying,” she said. “I left her like that.” Then, after a second, she added: “When he came to collect the children, he saw the boarding pass on the chest of drawers. He understood I was going to meet you.” Jacques wanted to take her hand. First he placed his fingers on the fabric of her coat, hesitated, then finally laid his hand on hers, gently, without squeezing. They drove on for a while. The driver kept his eyes on the road, indifferent. When they reached the building, Jacques paid, took the suitcase, and they went up. In the apartment, warmth enveloped them. The sea beyond the windows was black. Stéphanie slowly took off her shoes. Jacques offered to make tea. Stéphanie sat on the edge of the sofa. She looked at the sitting room, the large picture windows, the view, that silent luxury, and her face tightened for a second with exhaustion. She passed a hand quickly over her eyes. Jacques sat down beside her without touching her. He waited. At last Stéphanie turned her head towards him. “I’m sorry,” she said. “I can’t forget Juliette’s despair.” Jacques remained silent beside her. In the end, he got up to make tea.
