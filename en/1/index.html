Jacques had been walking through the streets of Stockholm for twenty minutes when he realised he was lost. The streets of Södermalm resembled one another, the façades remained mute, and a fine snow was falling without a sound. He checked the address on his phone one last time. Nothing indicated an entrance. He hesitated, retraced his steps, and eventually found a door. The air inside was warm. The heat carried scents with it, perfume, alcohol, the smell of skin. Music pulsed, not very loudly, but with an obstinate regularity, like a heart imitating life. Jacques took off his coat and handed it to a woman, who smiled back at him. He had no appetite for company, but forced himself to cross the first room. Women and men moved slowly through it. He recognised the dimmed light, the laughter, the bursts of voices, the glances offered and then withdrawn. Jacques spotted the bar, then, farther back, a second room. He exchanged two sentences in Swedish and took a flute of champagne someone handed him. He settled near a loudspeaker, his back against the wall. He watched. He thought again of the libertine nights in London. The crowd there had been louder, more festive, more hurried. Here, everything seemed restrained. Outside, the city seemed to go on watching over the bodies through the walls. He wandered, changed rooms, then came back. He already felt tired. The week had seemed empty to him, as it often had since his arrival. He moved through days that left nothing behind. He was being paid to wait. That was when he saw them. They occupied the centre of a larger room, and the space seemed to arrange itself around them. A young man, perhaps in his twenties, possessed an insolent, mythological beauty. A young Swedish woman, tall and blonde, had a diaphanous delicacy. They were making love without haste. There was a quiet necessity in their movements. Around them, people watched the scene, some with a gentle avidity, others with an almost scientific curiosity. The young man saw nothing. He was not performing. He was there, intent, fervent. For him, the world had shrunk to a patch of skin, a breath, a simple obstinacy of being alive. The young woman’s face disturbed Jacques. That face spoke of trust, surrender, something resembling love. They went on. Time stretched. They appeared to him naked yet without shame, strangers to the world around them. In that club saturated with gazes, they had, by some miracle, a scandalous innocence. Jacques felt a cold jealousy rise in him before the self-evidence that bound them. He watched their gestures as one watches, from the snow, a lit window, and knew himself to be exiled. Then the young man withdrew, and their embrace came undone. Afterwards, she took a handkerchief with a ceremonial gentleness. First, she wiped the forehead of her lover, damp with sweat, and set down that simple, intimate, domestic gesture, a gesture of home in the middle of the spectacle. Then she knelt and wiped what had fallen on the floor, without disgust, without haste, with calm attention. Only afterwards, with the same cloth, which she folded again, did she wipe her sex. Jacques remained motionless. The order of it struck him. He saw in it a tiny, household liturgy. The music continued. Around him, conversations resumed. He swallowed the rest of his champagne. The liquid seemed warm. He set the glass down. Then he turned back, retrieved his coat, thanked someone mechanically, and went out. Outside, the snow had started again. A white dust was falling patiently onto the pavements, the façades, the motionless cars. The cold bit into him. The warmth of the club went out all at once. His steps made a dull sound in the snow. He took out his phone. The screen lit up his fingers. For a moment he remained there, looking at that small, derisory light in the middle of the black city.
