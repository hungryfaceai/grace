Prologue
The Handkerchief **

Jacques had been walking for twenty minutes through the streets of Stockholm when he realized he was lost.
The streets of Södermalm all looked alike, the facades remained mute, while a fine snow fell without a sound. He checked the address on his phone one last time. Nothing indicated the entrance. He hesitated, retraced his steps, and finally found a door.
The air inside was mild. The warmth carried scents, alcohol, an odor of skin. The music throbbed, not very loudly, but with an obstinate regularity, like a heart imitating the living. Jacques took off his coat and handed it to a woman who smiled back at him.
He felt no social appetite, but forced himself to cross the first room. Women and men moved about slowly. He recognized the muted light, the laughter, the bursts of voices, the glances given and then withdrawn.
Jacques spotted the bar, then, at the back, a second room. He exchanged two sentences in Swedish and took the flute handed to him. He settled near a speaker, his back to the wall. He watched. He thought of the libertine nights in London. There the crowd had seemed noisier, more festive, more hurried. Here, everything felt held in check. The city outside seemed still to be watching over bodies through the walls.
He wandered, changed rooms, then came back. He already felt tired. The week had seemed empty to him, as it often did since his arrival. He was passing through days from which he kept nothing. He was being paid to wait.
That was when he saw them.
They occupied the center of a larger room, and the space seemed to arrange itself around them. A young man, perhaps in his twenties, bore an insolent, mythological beauty. A young Swedish woman, tall and blonde, had a diaphanous delicacy. They were making love without haste. There was in their movements a quiet necessity.
Around them, people stared at the scene, some with a gentle avidity, others with an almost scientific curiosity.
The young man, for his part, saw nothing. He was not performing. He was there, intent, fervent. For him, the world was reduced to a patch of skin, a breath, a simple stubbornness to live.
The young woman disturbed Jacques by her face. That face spoke of trust, surrender, something that resembled love. 
They went on. Time stretched.
They seemed to him naked but without shame, strangers to the world around them. In that club saturated with looks, they had, by some miracle, a scandalous innocence. Jacques felt a cold jealousy rise within him at the sight of the self-evidence that bound them. He watched their gestures as one watches, from the snow, a lit window, and he knew himself to be in exile.
Then the young man withdrew, and their embrace came undone.
Afterward, she took a handkerchief with ceremonial gentleness. First, she wiped her lover’s forehead, drenched in sweat, and made that simple, intimate, domestic gesture, a hearthside gesture in the middle of the spectacle. Then she knelt and wiped up what had fallen on the floor, without disgust, without haste, with calm attentiveness. And only afterward, with the same cloth, which she folded, she wiped her sex.
Jacques remained motionless. 
The order of it struck him. He saw in it a tiny, housewifely liturgy.
The music went on. Conversations resumed all around.
He swallowed the rest of his flute. The liquid seemed lukewarm to him. He set down the glass. Then he turned on his heel, collected his coat, thanked them mechanically, and went out.
Outside, the snow had started up again. A white dust was settling patiently once more on the sidewalks, on the facades, on the motionless cars. The cold bit him. The warmth of the club went out all at once. His footsteps made a dull sound in the snow. He took out his phone. The screen lit his fingers. For a moment he stood looking at that small, derisory light in the middle of the black city.

Chapter 1
Consensus **

On Monday morning, Jacques woke at seven. He drank three cups of coffee while looking out at the frozen water from the living room of the sumptuous three-room apartment he had rented in Östermalm, facing Nybroviken. He thought about the week ahead.
The phone vibrated on the coffee table, screen against the wood, like a small impatient creature. Jacques turned it over. Stéphanie had written early, from London.
“Did you do anything this weekend? Did you go out?”
He stared at the question for a moment, so simple. He thought of the club, of the handkerchief above all, and of the disturbance he had felt rising inside him. He had the absurd urge to tell her everything, to unburden himself.
He typed: “I went…”
Then he deleted it.
He typed again: “Not much.”
He hesitated again, added a sentence, deleted that too. In the end he sent:
“I stayed in. I miss you.”
When the message went, his shoulders sagged. He put the phone away. He told himself he would explain later. 
He pulled on his long coat, gloves, and wool hat, and went out. He strode down Strandvägen, caught in the February cold. He hurried toward his office.
Ever since arriving at the Swedish investment bank where he had signed on for a year, he had been asked for his opinion on everything. No decision seemed complete until it had been “validated” by an Anglo-Saxon consultant paid a fortune. Most of them worked no more than two hours a day, with the solemnity of priests in a godless cult.
Jacques set down his coat, got himself another coffee, opened his computer. In his inbox, three invitations to “catch-ups” had piled up overnight. He accepted them without reading, by reflex.
In his mind, he arranged the chairs for the weekly meeting. He already knew half the team would be missing. The English and the Germans arrived on Mondays as one joins a distant war, in waves, from London and Frankfurt, between eleven and four. The last, coming from Luxembourg, treated himself, or rather had himself treated, to the luxury of starting his week at five o’clock, invoking the lack of a direct flight. Sometimes Jacques even crossed paths with him at the entrance while, in the elevator, Swedes were already heading back down toward their homes, with the peaceful air of people whose day is done.
The head of department had ruled: the meeting would remain at nine. For a moment she had considered organizing one every hour of the day, then given it up. At nine, Jacques joined his Swedish colleagues in the conference room. They were recounting their weekend of family cross-country skiing, children bundled in multicolored snowsuits, hauled in little sleds to the point of exhaustion.
The head of the department was named Alma. On the first day, Jacques had thought she must once have been very beautiful. After thirty-four years in banking, she ran IT research and development with that dry energy of survivors, that way of remaining upright in the middle of ruins.
She began, as always, with sobriety.
“We need a consensus.”
Consensus, in this bank, had the status of a mythological creature. Everyone talked about it, no one had ever seen it, and as soon as one approached its natural habitat, it vanished into the underbrush of “dependencies” and “unapproved budget.” Slides were projected. Someone asked what exactly “consensus” meant in the context. Someone else pointed out that the budget had not been approved. A third spoke of dependencies with the same gravity as an addiction specialist. Voices overlapped, then died away, then returned, each defending its little patch of territory. Jacques watched their hands: those fidgeting with a pen, those smoothing the corner of a sheet of paper, those typing a message under the table.
Consensus did not occur. It never did.
Jacques had already stopped listening. The evening two days earlier came back to him in fragments: skin, muted light, and that handkerchief above all. He preferred not to share his thoughts with the team. Alma would do very well without his ideas.
Jacques remembered that the colleague sitting beside him had nearly strangled another one the week before, and he assented to each of his sentences with a cautious nod. Independent consultants like him drew, as a bonus, a more muffled animosity, as though their mere presence silently reminded everyone of the failure to manage without them.
An hour later, each person was leaving the meeting hating a little more those with whom he had failed to agree. Alma, for her part, had that satisfying impression of having made progress, which is to say of having shifted, without resolving them, the same problems from one week to the next.
At eleven in the morning, the Swedish colleagues drifted out in clusters to go eat lunch downstairs. Jacques had declined the invitation on the first day; it was never renewed. The office emptied. A peace settled in.
Jacques sat down on a sofa, took out his iPhone 7, and gave himself over to his favorite pastime: sliding his index finger from left to right as fast as possible. Every so often, a woman’s face appeared: smiling promise, first name, quotation. Jacques pasted the same message each time, with the quiet efficiency of a zealous employee.
After twenty-five minutes, the profiles ran dry. The screen flickered, then offered the dizzying void. There were no more available candidates for the moment.
He laid the phone face down on the sofa.
He went out for a walk as far as Kungsträdgården. Outside, the air cut like a blade. He came back an hour later, sat at his workstation, and returned to work with impeccable seriousness. It was two in the afternoon; most of his English colleagues had just settled in at their stations.
Doctor Fynn came over to him.
“How are you, Jacques? Did you stay in Stockholm over the weekend? What did you do?”
Jacques nodded.
“Not much,” he answered vaguely. 
“My partner, Stéphanie, is divorced. She had to take care of her children. She stayed in London, as often happens. She didn’t come join me.”
“How did the meeting go this morning? Did you mention my new model to Alma?”
“No,” Jacques replied. He had not remembered.
Fynn was a well-known mathematician, one of those who had managed to convert solid academic ability into a lucrative career. He had made a fortune implementing statistical trading algorithms at a New York hedge fund. Back in London, he had bought himself a splendid Edwardian house in Hammersmith, facing the Thames, beneath the continuous rumble of planes approaching Heathrow airport from four in the morning onward.
As he spoke, he worried at his tie with methodical nervousness, to the point that it had become worn. He wore trousers that were too short, exposing scuffed Tod’s shoes unsuited to Stockholm snow; every time he went outside, he covered them with little transparent plastic caps that greatly amused Jacques. 
His work consisted in putting a price on the future. He called it a model. He tried to seize unstable quantities, contaminated by man, by his panics, his greed, and his cunning. He assumed uncertainty followed a law. For a long time Jacques had believed in the usefulness of his profession. He was no longer so sure.
Jacques remembered London, eight years earlier. He had worked on assets linked to credit risk. He had been astonished to see the systems developed by the bank’s researchers produce such extravagant valuations. Behind the screens, there had been a false serenity.
One day, he had taken the elevator up to talk to research about it. 
“Who are you, then? Ah, the frog downstairs? Next time, make an appointment. Are you a PhD? Don’t try to understand. Don’t even try.”
Jacques had gone back down to the basement. Even so, he had tried to understand, a little, tapping on his first-generation iPhone. Copula, Google had answered him affably.
He had gone on coding.
Six months later, he had gone upstairs to present his system to the credit desk. They had kept him waiting. He had asked where his users were. No one seemed to know. He had insisted, politely. 
Then he had come upon an obese man in his thirties, a box in one hand, a beer in the other. The shirt strained over his belly.
“Do you know where the credit desk sits?” Jacques had inquired politely.
“You’re late, mate. I’m the last one. And they’ve just fired me too.”
He had smiled as he said it, without bitterness, with that thoroughly British good nature. He shrugged, roguish, and added: 
“Pack up your software. Grab a box. There are some over there.”
And for an instant Jacques had seen himself standing there with his computer, his seriousness, and his useless equations, in the middle of a collapse that people simply called “restructuring.”
Chapter 2
The arrivals hall **

Three days later, on Thursday evening, Jacques left the office at four o’clock. He picked up his computer, buttoned his coat, crossed the open-plan office with that mixture of discretion and haste which, in open-plan offices, arouses suspicion. In the elevator, he felt joyful at the thought of seeing Stéphanie again for a few days. Her children were with their father until Monday.
Outside, it was already dark. He hailed a Tesla taxi. The cabin smelled of new leather and cutting-edge technology. The driver asked no questions. Jacques merely said: Arlanda.
They drove through Vasastan. Then the city center faded away. The facades grew lower, the lights thinned out, and the road unrolled ahead. Jacques took out his iPhone, then put it away again at once. He fussed over absurd details: what he would say when he saw her, how he would place his hand on her suitcase, whether he should kiss her straightaway or wait. He had the same nervousness as before an important meeting.
He thought of London. In summer, people linger, the pubs overflow, the parks become makeshift beaches. Stéphanie came back to him first through her sweet scent, then through that face marked by a silent kindness.
She was raising two children alone in London, in a small flat overlooking Cleveland Square, with its carefully kept garden in the middle, the kind one finds in the prosperous neighborhoods of the city’s west end. She had separated from her husband a year before meeting Jacques.
He saw again their first night together. She had invited him to her place one summer evening, windows open onto the small square garden, and they had watched Leviathan, that Russian film.
He could still see the sea: a cold shoreline, rusted sheets of metal, and, on the beach, the white carcass of a whale. The story hinged on very little: a piece of coveted land, and a man being told that what was his would be taken from him.
Stéphanie had murmured, barely moving: 
“They’re going to crush him.”
Jacques had nodded. The court reeled off its ruling, the police smiled, the friends drank, and the Church stood there, motionless. Then their bodies had sought each other, and the film had gone on behind them.
They had met too late for naive beginnings, yet still early enough to believe another life might remain possible. Jacques was thirty-eight. She was forty. 
She had spoken to him at length about her children, the difficult logistics of family life, and what she called the lack of human warmth. He also knew, through hints and silences, that her departure had been an explosion in Stéphanie’s life and in the children’s. But he did not know why. Stéphanie had never wanted to speak of it. He had not questioned her, waiting for her to open up.
The taxi was nearing Arlanda. The signs lined up, the exits came one after another. Jacques felt his stomach tighten. He was about to see her again, and the thought made him feel light.
In the arrivals hall, the airport heat was stifling. Travelers dragged wheeled suitcases, their eyes tired. Jacques stood near the automatic doors. He waited.
When she appeared, he did not recognize her at once. She seemed thinner and more strained. She was holding her phone in one hand, her suitcase in the other. She looked around for Jacques, and when she saw him, something relaxed in her face, for barely a second, and then that restraint he had first perceived returned.
They drew closer. He was about to say something awkward, one word too many, a pointless joke. He opened his mouth, then fell silent. Stéphanie forestalled him. She placed a hand on his sleeve, a brief touch, sure, verifying.
“I’m here,” she said.
Jacques held her for a long time. He took her hand, and they walked toward the exit.
In the taxi, on the way back, they talked about the weekend’s plans. Jacques suggested going skiing at Hellasgården. Stockholm slipped by. Jacques looked at Stéphanie. She was staring at the black road, her hands resting motionless on her bag. He wanted to lay a hand on her, draw her against him.
Stéphanie inhaled, then exhaled slowly. Jacques thought she was going to talk about the week. Instead, she said:
“I had my daughter on the phone. Just before boarding. She was crying.”
Jacques moved closer to her.
“What’s wrong?”
Stéphanie hesitated for a second. Then she spoke in a controlled voice, without pathos.
“Their father told them... He told them: ‘Do you know where your mother is? She’s in Sweden this weekend. Anything could happen to you.’”
She paused.
“She’s seven years old. She heard that and imagined... she imagined I was abandoning them, her and her little brother. That something could happen to them and I would not be there. She cried as though someone were pushing her into a hole.”
Jacques felt a dull anger rising in him. 
Stéphanie went on, more softly:
“I kept repeating that I was coming back. That I was not leaving them. That I loved them. She was still crying. She wanted me to promise things... impossible things. That nothing would ever happen to them. That she would never be sad. That she would never be alone.”
She turned her eyes toward the window. The city slid past, and on the glass lights were reflected that looked like cracks.
“I left her in tears,” she said. “I left her like that.”
Then she added, after a second:
“When he came to pick up the children, he saw the boarding pass on the chest of drawers. He understood that I was leaving to come and join you.”
Jacques wanted to take her hand. He first laid his fingers on the fabric of her coat, hesitated, then at last placed his hand over hers, gently, without squeezing.
They drove on a while longer. The driver kept his eyes on the road, indifferent. 
When they reached the building, Jacques paid, took the suitcase, and they went up. In the flat, warmth enveloped them. The sea beyond the windows was black. Stéphanie took off her shoes slowly. Jacques offered to make tea.
Stéphanie sat down on the edge of the sofa. She looked at the living room, the large bay windows, the view, that silent luxury, and her face tightened for a second with exhaustion. She passed a hand quickly over her eyes.
Jacques sat down beside her, without touching her. He waited.
At last Stéphanie turned her head toward him.
“I’m sorry,” she said. “I can’t stop thinking about my daughter Julie’s despair.”
Jacques remained silent at her side. In the end he got up to go and make tea.
Chapter 3
The Black Scabbardfish **

Stéphanie fell asleep early that evening. Jacques opened an Omnipollo beer, then a second, and finally a third, without finding in them the taste of rest. Around midnight, he went to join her in the bedroom and slipped in beside her.
On Saturday, they got up late. Jacques suggested a walk to Djurgården before nightfall.
“Come on. It’ll take your mind off things.”
Stéphanie’s eyes, usually so merry, flew open with a childlike joy. Jacques felt his heart grow warm.
They walked for a long time, aimlessly, through the royal park. The trees were bare. The grass was white. At times, Stéphanie stopped and stood facing the sea. 
They went into a small café. Stéphanie smiled, then her face closed again, just slightly, with the tiniest crease at the corner of her eye. She had received a message. She looked at the screen without opening it right away, then set the phone face down on the table.
“Is it your daughter?” Jacques asked.
Stéphanie nodded.
“She’s better,” she said, without conviction. “She’s stopped crying. But she asked me if I was coming back. Twice.”
Jacques felt helpless. He thought of the father’s remark, of the abject nature of his outburst. He said nothing about it. 
They kept walking. Parents were pulling sleds. The children laughed, sending their shrill cries into the white air. Stéphanie watched them, and Jacques had the impression she was looking at something else as well, a fulfilled family life she might have had, or had believed she held.
On the way back, they picked up a few things at the supermarket. Jacques bought salmon, black bread, and fruit. In the apartment, he set the bags on the counter, switched on a lamp, and the room suddenly showed itself to be welcoming. Stéphanie took off her sweater. She went into the bathroom and washed her hands for a long time. Jacques watched her without interrupting.
Then she sat down on the sofa, legs drawn up, the phone on her knees. She sent a message, received another, and replied again. 
By the time she finally set the phone down, Stockholm was already sunk in darkness. She closed her eyes for a moment.
“I’m sorry,” she said. “I’m useless.”
Jacques shook his head.
“You’re tired. That’s all.”
She opened her eyes again and held his gaze. Between them, there was a second of silence.
Jacques moved closer. He sat beside her, without touching her at first. He felt his own body, the desire, the fear of doing the wrong thing.
Stéphanie laid her hand on his with a cautious gesture. She had that composed face he knew, that adult face that refuses to complain. 
Jacques kissed her. She did not respond at once. Then, slowly, she relaxed. Her body moved nearer, hesitantly.
They went toward the bedroom without speaking. Jacques was afraid of breaking something, and that apprehension made him gentler.
In the bedroom, Stéphanie stopped beside the bed. For a moment she looked at the window, at the night, then turned toward him. Jacques undid the buttons of her shirt. He did not hurry. He touched her with deliberate slowness. Beneath his fingers, he felt the tension yield in places, return, then yield again. She closed her eyes, and a deeper breath ran through her body.
When they came together, it was a return. Jacques felt the warmth rise again, the impassioned love of summer, but transformed, less impatient. There was something recovered in it, and something new. She clung to him with a silent force.
They stayed like that for a long time, until weariness caught up with them. Jacques felt the sweat on his skin, the crumpled sheet, and the distant noise of the city.
Stéphanie got up first. She came back with a glass of water. She set it on the bedside table, then sat on the edge of the bed. She gently stroked Jacques’s hair. The gesture was simple. And yet he felt a discreet shock as he thought again of the club he had not told her about.
Stéphanie slipped under the blanket and lay still for a moment, her eyes open in the dark.
She was going to speak. He did not dare help her to it. He waited.
“I’m going to tell you something,” she said at last.
“I never explained to you why my husband and I separated.”
Jacques did not answer. He simply laid his hand on her thigh. Stéphanie drew a breath.
“I thought it was an ordinary story of betrayal. I thought I had been cheated on, that was all. I thought there was another woman, that the love between us had worn thin after our boy was born. I told myself it happens. I would have cried, I would have been humiliated, but I would have survived.”
She gave a small laugh full of melancholy.
“But it wasn’t that.”
She was looking at the ceiling. The room was dark, and yet Jacques had the impression he could see her very clearly. He held his breath.
“One evening, his phone lit up in the dark. I simply caught sight of a notification. I picked it up, without thinking. And I read.”
She broke off.
“I saw a man’s first name. Under it there was a sentence too intimate to mistake. I read it three times. Not because I doubted. Because my brain could not manage to understand what it was reading.”
Jacques felt his throat tighten. Stéphanie went on in the same gentle, precise voice.
“I didn’t make a scene. I didn’t even shout. The floor gave way beneath my feet. And I felt an immense sadness, at the thought that I had spent all that time living beside a stranger.”
She turned her head toward Jacques. Her eyes were shining, but she was not crying.
“What finished me was that everything about him was coherent. He came from Poitou, like me. He had climbed the social ladder. He had given us that standard of living that silences doubt: the big flat near Bayswater, on Moscow Road, holidays in Porquerolles, bilingual school for our children. I took that for proof of love and solidity. I believed solidity replaced everything else.”
She closed her eyes for a moment.
“He had been leading a double life for years. There had been no lapse. No accident. The construction was perfect. The lie had patiently settled in.”
Jacques’s mouth went dry.
Stéphanie resumed, and her voice grew more precise.
“He even invited his lover to our home one evening, passing him off as a colleague. I nodded along. I did what I always do. I wanted everything to be right.”
A silence passed.
Then she said, very softly:
“I cooked marinated black scabbardfish with miso and saké. You know, Nobu’s recipe.”
Jacques looked at her.
“All evening long, I smiled. I wanted to be perfect. I wanted the guest to feel at ease, for my husband to be proud of me.”
She swallowed.
“And they had those silences. They exchanged quick smiles. Their gazes lingered. I felt it clearly that evening, without understanding. I tidied up afterward. I washed the glasses and the plates. Like an idiot.”
A first tear finally ran down. She did not wipe it away.
“When I read the messages later, it all came back to me in a single blow. And I understood that I hadn’t only cooked. I had set the scene. I had prepared the backdrop for my own humiliation.”
Stéphanie went on, more quietly:
“It isn’t just that he betrayed me. It’s that he... he never loved me the way I believed. He loved a facade. A normality. He played that role. And I, in the end, loved alone.”
At last she turned her face toward Jacques. Tears were running down her cheeks. She was sobbing.
“Since then, I don’t know how to trust anymore. Even when I want to. Even when I love you.”
Jacques took her in his arms. He did not speak. He did not look for some phrase to set things right. He simply held her.
Stéphanie rested her forehead against his shoulder. Her breathing broke for a second, then returned. She murmured:
“Tonight, I tried.”
Jacques kissed her hair. 
In the dark, he heard his phone vibrate, a distant notification. He did not move.
Stéphanie drew back a little, just enough to look at him.
“Will you promise me one thing?” she asked.
Jacques nodded.
“Promise me that if one day you don’t want to go on, you’ll tell me?”
He answered, without emphasis:
“I’ll tell you.”
Chapter 4
The solstice **

In June, in Stockholm, night no longer truly fell. It retreated, dissolved, and left behind a dawn-like pallor. Facing Nybroviken, the water had lost February’s blackness. At last it moved again, scored with quick reflections. The gulls cried above the boats, demanding their share of the season.
Four months earlier, Jacques had thought he had found a balance. Then the balance had revealed itself to be a pose. Stéphanie and he had drifted apart without any crash or uproar. There had been no scene, not even any final words. She came to Sweden less often; Jacques’s trips to London had grown rarer. He noticed that the child-custody calendar she shared with him was no longer being updated. He answered her messages with less eagerness.
With the fine weather, Stockholm seemed to widen. Nature and human beings were returning to themselves. Café terraces spilled over the sidewalks; glasses could be heard clinking late into the night. The parks filled with bodies stretched out on the grass; skin offered itself to the all too rare sun. In Hagaparken, groups were stretching, barefoot, bare-shouldered, music playing softly. Farther on, the forest began. You could take the metro, walk ten minutes, and find yourself beside a motionless lake, ringed with pines. The air there smelled of resin.
At weekends, posters announced open-air concerts, temporary stages on the quays, orchestras in the parks. Even people seemed to speak more loudly, as though winter’s silence had left them with a fear of vanishing.
Jacques still worked. Or rather, he went to the office.
The bank had not changed. He moved through empty days where his role was reduced to being a seated body. Summer made this vacuity even more humiliating, because meanwhile all the Swedes were enjoying two months of vacation. He regularly went from floor to floor to make sure the desertion was general. Even the high command had withdrawn to the sommarstugor, those little summer cottages passed down from generation to generation.
He felt exhausted by the absence of effort. 
There was, however, one notable exception. Toward the end of the month, his mind would suddenly wake. His attention fixed itself on numbers with a kind of mystical intensity. Then he recovered an energy he had never had in the preceding weeks, and reserved it for this administrative ritual.
On this June 21, he opened his file, the one he always reused for the accounts of the company he had founded in London six years earlier, LatentEdge.
He counted the days on which he had given body and soul.
Twenty-one.
He typed:
“Daily rate: £1,200 per day.”
He did the calculation slowly, and checked it several times, for fear of making a mistake:
21 × £1,200.
He wrote: £25,200.
The Excel sheet automatically added VAT. The total came to £30,240 for the month of June. He added the rent for his apartment, which he had negotiated separately: SEK 53,000; that is, £4,758.
The same spreadsheet carried out the addition with diligence: £34,998.
The sum gave him a brief, mechanical satisfaction. It had the purity of an exact result. A month had just taken shape.
That evening, he came home very late. It was still not dark. Stockholm refused to offer him that relief. The light persisted, stubbornly.
In the apartment, he closed the curtains, then poured himself a glass of water, sat down, and opened the dating app. He liked the repetitive gesture. Above all, he liked the absence of immediate consequence. One could make contact without compromising oneself, and disappear without explanation. 
He scrolled through the profiles.
One face arrested his finger. A woman wore a broad smile that revealed very white teeth. She was wearing a green and yellow bikini on a tropical beach.
Manuela.
Bio: “Brazilian. Coffee, museums, long walks. Relationship goal: still thinking about it. Still figuring out,” said the app.
He swiped the picture to the right.
Match.
She replied quickly:
“Shall we grab a coffee this week?”
He said yes. They settled on Thursday, six o’clock, near Odenplan, in a café where you could sit outside.
Chapter 5
The Clock and the Latch **

Thursday, at six o’clock, in June, Stockholm still looked as if it were the middle of the afternoon. The city had bare skin. The light refused to leave bodies in peace. The hour no longer mattered.
Jacques arrived early. He sat down at a table and watched. People were leaving work without haste. Bicycles skimmed past the tables. 
He watched a great deal now.

Le Petit Moulin **

“That’s false.”
Jacques looked at the screen.
“False?”
“Well, not completely.”

“No, I know what the screen says. I’m asking what is actually happening.”

“Fine. Off the record. Then tell me where to look.”

“Do you have to write right away?” he asked.
“Yes. Now, before the dead sentence wins.”
She turned back to the keyboard. This time, he really watched her work.

“Morning. One question. Is this linked to the outage, or are people just using the outage to push their agenda?”

Jacques smiled.
“And you do.”
“That’s not it. I can still do it. But it isn’t the same job anymore.”

“My job is no longer to obtain. It is to keep the false from taking the shape of the true.”
“Is it harder?” Jacques asked.
She shrugged.
“I don’t know. It’s less visible. Before, you could see the effort. Now, if I do my job well, it mostly feels as though there was nothing to correct.”
He joined her by the window. In the pane, he caught sight of their reflection superimposed on the still empty street. He drew her to him.
“And yet you keep getting up at dawn,” he said.
This time she smiled openly.
“Yes.”
On the counter, her phone vibrated again. Her New York editor was forwarding her a note generated during the night with this remark: can you humanize this?
She looked at the screen, then turned it face down on the table.
Jacques loved her more since he had seen her work like that. Not because she was brilliant, nor because she resisted a new age better than others. But because amid all this she had kept the desire to go and verify.

The Test

"Yes. Sorry. I've only just read your messages."

"You..."

"Yes."

"You don't look happy."

"Of course I am."

She lowered her eyes for a moment, then raised her head again.

"How far along?"
"I don't know exactly. Five weeks."
"Have you seen anyone?"
"I called my medical insurance during the day. I have an appointment next Friday."
She said it simply, with her habitual efficiency.
"It happened when you got back from Zagreb, on Sunday, remember?" she said, smiling.

He had stayed to work in London, then had spent the weekend in Zagreb. He had stayed there in an apartment in the old town, with high ceilings, large windows, and a view onto a steep cobbled street. He had promised himself he would come home less late. Naturally, he had arrived exhausted and frozen. They had made love quickly, then fallen asleep.

She had read a great deal, knew the numbers, the timelines, the probabilities of conceiving in the early forties. They had stopped all contraception a few months earlier, during a trip to Iceland. Jacques would not have been able to say at exactly what moment his reluctance had fallen away. They had spent the afternoon around the glacier of Svinafellsjokull. The ice came down between the dark slopes in broad frozen folds, streaked with ash, blue in places with a dense blue, like light imprisoned beneath the surface.
They had left too late for dinner. That evening they had eaten the sandwiches they had left, then made love with a youthful and reckless fervor, like two beings who, for the first time, were consenting without reserve to what was to come.
"I hadn't expected to be pregnant so quickly," she said.
"Neither had I."
He listened to her.
He wanted to stand up, go to her, take her in his arms. Yet he remained seated, held back by something grave. He felt a deep shift within himself. His life had just lost, in a single sentence, part of its reversible character. Until now, almost everything could still be undone, altered, left behind. A city, an assignment, a woman sometimes, a habit, a future. Even love, so long as no child had passed through it, kept a secret exit. Now that door was closing without violence, but with certainty.
He thought of the end of that silent freedom that had long served him as a spine, the possibility of disappearing and leaving the future in a deliberately provisional state. That world was withdrawing.
"What are you thinking about?" she asked.
"Too many things at once."
She waited, then finally placed her hand on his. The contact was cool. Jacques lowered his eyes to their joined fingers.
"You don't have to react right away," she said.
After a few seconds, Jacques finally stood up, went around the table, and knelt beside her. He pressed his head against her belly. Through the fabric of her dress, he felt only the warmth of her body and the simplicity of her presence.
Lyubov ran a hand through his hair.
"There's still nothing to hear," she said.
"I know."
Even so, he remained like that for a few moments.
When he stood up again, her eyes were shining slightly. He did not know whether she was about to cry. But the tears did not come.
Later, they sat down on the sofa without turning on the television. Lyubov had nestled against him, her legs drawn up beneath the blanket brought back from a trip to Scotland. He offered to make tea. She agreed.
They spoke little. A few sentences about the appointment to come, about the weeks during which they would still have to keep quiet, about how one day they would announce it to those they loved. Then, little by little, the words withdrew.
At eleven o'clock, she said she was going to sleep.
In bed, she fell asleep before he did, as always.
Jacques lay on his back looking at the darkness. An invisible being had just entered the world without knowing, without language, without defense, and was already demanding from them an attention that no method could assume in their place.
He also thought, with a lucidity free of pathos, that he would never again be alone in quite the same way. Even if he one day lost his job, even if the world reorganized itself around machines, even if society as a whole slid toward a form of generalized assistance, there would now be this happy and terrible debt: he would no longer be able to live as if his presence in the world remained optional.
He turned his head toward the sleeping Lyubov. In the dark, he could barely make out her profile on the pillow. He laid his hand on the sheet, level with her belly, not touching her at first, then very lightly.

The Perimeter

Two months later, on a Tuesday morning, Jacques arrived early at Canary Wharf.
He had slept badly. Lyubov, for her part, had been sleeping more heavily for the past few days. Before leaving, she had asked whether he would be home in time for dinner. He had said he thought so.
The sky hung low. In the lobby, the screens were broadcasting market prices. Jacques passed through the gates and went up to his floor as usual.

"Can you come by at 10?"
Jacques looked at the message for a few seconds. Nothing in it indicated the importance of the meeting. And yet for several weeks he had known that a moment of this kind would eventually come. The mapping was complete. The last workshops had taken place. The transition plans were now circulating among management, human resources, and platform leads. Where, two years earlier, people had spoken of transformation, they now spoke of execution.
He replied simply:
"Yes."
Until ten o'clock, he worked on the summary of a confirmations flow that had become almost entirely autonomous. They all led to the same conclusion. Human intervention had not disappeared, but it had become residual, scattered, harder to justify in terms of headcount. Entire teams were no longer needed. All that was needed was a small number of people capable of monitoring, correcting exceptions, and bearing legal responsibility.

Peter stood up to shake his hand, which he did not ordinarily do when they saw each other at the office. 
"Come in."

"I'll get straight to the point," he said at last.
Jacques nodded.

"Yes."

Chapter 30
The First Ultrasound

The appointment had been set for eight fifty, in a clinic in Knightsbridge, very near the Russian Orthodox Cathedral of the Dormition. Its Lombard facade rose like a fragment of old Russia lost in the English calm of Ennismore Gardens.
In the lobby, everything had been designed to reassure. On the wall screen appeared the names of the practitioners and the waiting times. Jacques immediately saw in it the mark of his age. The patient agrees to real-time algorithmic assistance during the examination. Human clinical validation is mandatory.

“You’ll see right away if everything is all right?”

The discussion continued. They spoke of governance, of an external ethics committee, and of a former regulator who might accompany them. Every safeguard seemed reasonable. Violence never presented itself naked. It arrived clothed in exceptions, guardrails, clauses, and good intentions.

Jacques spoke little. He listened to the agreement taking shape around him.
At one point, Maya asked him:
“What worries you most?”
“That we might be good.”
Maya narrowed her eyes slightly.
“I’m sorry?”
“If Sentiens is often wrong, the market will correct us. If the tool is crude, people will mistrust it. But if it becomes good, truly good, then it will be much harder to resist.”
Adrian stared at the table.
Vivian remained motionless.
Jacques continued.
“The danger is that the machine will see accurately enough for people to agree to forget everything it does not see.”
Maya did not answer at once. For the first time since the meeting had begun, she seemed less an investor than the attentive reader of a sentence she did not yet know what to do with.
Simon Vale broke the silence.
“That is precisely why this product must be built with people who think that way.”
At the end, Maya closed her folder.
“We are not asking for an answer today.”
They stood. Hands were shaken. Eleanor promised to send a term sheet on Monday. Maya accompanied them to the elevator.
“Take time to talk it over among yourselves,” she said.
In the elevator, no one said anything. Vivian was watching the numbers descending above the door. Adrian had his eyes closed. Jacques could make out his reflection in the metal wall.
Vivian drew a deep breath.
“It’s a good offer.”
Jacques walked a few steps without speaking.
“They want to strip out Naptio,” he said at last.
She did not answer at once. Jacques read compassion on her face.
“Jacques, childhood cannot be our brand strategy.”
“If we want Sentiens to survive, we have to make it investable.”
Vivian resumed, more gently:
“Listen to me. If we refuse, what happens? We go on with limited means. Another player will do the same thing with fewer scruples. Sentiens will not stop this world from arriving. But perhaps we can decide some of its limits.”
Jacques knew the argument. He had heard it in other lives, under other forms. If it isn’t us, it will be worse. The world is moving forward; we might as well hold the wheel. He had always found that sentence reasonable. He felt himself yielding.
“I need to talk to Lyubov,” he said.
Vivian nodded.
“Of course.”
Jacques did not reply.
That evening, he came home later than expected. The apartment was quiet. Lyubov was sitting in the living room, her legs folded beneath her, her computer open on her lap. A bluish light lit her face. On the coffee table lay a picture book, a tiny sock, two blocks, and a cup of cold tea.
She looked up.
“Well?”
Jacques took off his coat, laid it over the back of a chair, then remained standing for a moment, not knowing where to begin.
“They want to take an equity stake.”
Lyubov slowly closed her computer.
“How much?”
“Four million.”
She showed no surprise.
“For what share?”
“Eighteen percent. Fully diluted. Twenty-two million post-money. A little over eighteen pre-money.”
She looked at him attentively.
“You’ve already done the calculation.”
“Yes.”
She pointed to the armchair opposite her. He sat down. In the bedroom, Thomas was already asleep. The door stood ajar. A night-light cast a faint glow over the hallway floorboards.
Lyubov asked:
“And what do they want?”
Jacques passed his hands over his face.
He added:
“They want Sentiens to become an enterprise platform.”
“To read employees.”
“To help organizations detect human signals.”
“Jacques.”
He lowered his eyes.
“Yes. To read employees. Not officially. Not individually. Not directly. But yes.”
Lyubov remained silent.
“You want to say yes,” she said at last.
Jacques would have liked to answer otherwise. He would have liked to put forward the guardrails, the governance, the ethics. All that was true, but all that was incomplete.
“Yes,” he said.
She received the word without moving.
“Why?”
He searched for a long time.
“Because it’s useful. Because it could become important. Because I know how to do this. Because they saw something.”
“In you or in the product?”
The question surprised him.
“Both, perhaps.”
Lyubov turned her head toward the hallway. Behind the door, Thomas slept, absent from this negotiation that had nevertheless arisen from his existence.
“After your last contract, you thought the world had taken away your value,” she said.
He felt irritation rise, then subside at once. She was touching a wound he would have preferred to keep secret.
“It isn’t only that.”
“I know.”
“Sentiens can help. Really. In hospitals, emergency teams, call centers, specialized schools.”
“And it can also give a manager a reason no longer to trust a face.”
“Managers won’t see individual data.”
“Today.”
Jacques fell silent.
Lyubov resumed more gently:
“I’m not asking you to refuse because I’m afraid.”
He raised his eyes.
“But?”
“But don’t tell me it’s only care.”
“You may want to succeed. You may want to prove to them that they were wrong to throw you out. You may want to be admired. You may want Thomas to be proud of you one day. You may want money too. All of that is human. But if you call all of that care, then you will lose yourself.”
Jacques remained motionless.
In the bedroom, Thomas stirred. A small cry rose, brief, still caught in sleep. Jacques stood up reflexively. Lyubov laid a hand on his arm.
“Wait.”
They listened. Silence returned. Then a steady breathing came faintly through the door.
Jacques remained standing. His phone vibrated on the coffee table. The screen lit up.
Vivian.
Eleanor will send the term sheet Monday. Maya wants an answer in principle before the end of the week. We have to move forward.
Lyubov saw the light of the message on his face. She did not ask to read it.
Jacques took the phone, then looked at the bedroom door. The night-light drew a pale band across the floorboards. Beyond it, Thomas slept, his fists closed, still free to know nothing of what was coming toward him.
The phone vibrated a second time in his hand.
Jacques did not answer. He simply remained there, in the living room crossed by the child’s light, between the money that was calling and the breathing that continued behind the door.
Chapter 37
The Forehead

Two and a half years passed.
Thomas had just turned five. He was now very tall for his age. His long blond curls fell over his cheeks. His face had grown longer. His cheeks had lost the roundness of his earliest years. His eyes still held that brief gravity he had had as a newborn when he stared at adults, trying to decipher their curious antics.
He now spoke in a mixed language in which Jacques’s French, Lyubov’s Russian, and London’s English competed with one another. He said certain things in French when he played with his father; others in English when they belonged to school. Russian remained the language of tenderness and confidences with his mother.
Sentiens had not survived.
At first, though, the company had succeeded. It had hired its hundredth employee a year earlier and continued raising funds. The pilots had been excellent. The aggregated dashboards pleased executive committees. Consulting firms, call centers, two networks of private clinics, and a major insurance group had signed on. The first results had been presented at a conference in Amsterdam. Vivian had spoken of prevention and dignity. The whole audience had applauded.
Then there had been the affair.
An employee at a call center in the north of England, near Liverpool, had thrown himself off a bridge into the River Mersey. He was thirty-seven years old. He was the father of two children, aged four and six.
His name circulated in the press for several months.
The man had left a letter for his wife, who read it in full before the media. In it, he blamed Sentiens. He denounced the pressure of targets, harassment from his superiors, recorded calls, weekly rankings. His manager had told him he was not sufficiently committed. He lived in constant fear of losing his job. He was struggling to repay the mortgage on a small house for which it had taken him years to gather the initial deposit. The elder of his children had autism, and his wife had left her job in a supermarket to devote more time to him.
In that accumulation of injustices that life had hurled in his face, one sentence had been enough to overturn everything.
“They even knew when I was pretending to be all right.”
The sentence was repeated in every media outlet. It targeted Sentiens, which his employer had integrated into its call-center system. People spoke of a suicide brought on by surveillance. Of a man whose distress had been seen, measured, classified, but never heard.
Screenshots circulated. They showed curves, orange zones, disengagement alerts. It came out that managers did not have access to individual data, but that they sometimes received cohorts small enough to guess. It came out that consent had been folded opaquely into employment contracts and that employees opted into the use of Sentiens by default. It was pointed out that employees did not really have an alternative and rarely refused tools their employer presented as optional.
The tragedy eventually triggered a parliamentary inquiry committee that was broadcast on television.
Jacques watched Vivian defend the company on television. Her face was pale and her shoulders straight. She repeated that Sentiens had never made a disciplinary decision, that the tool was not designed to identify individuals, and that the client had violated the spirit of their contract. The technology had been perverted from its original use, she hammered out.
All of that was true.
All of that saved nothing.
Then Vivian disappeared from interviews.
For a few days, her name circulated alongside Jacques’s. Then Sentiens entrusted its communications to a public-relations firm, which took over the press releases and interview requests. Vivian published only a very short message. In it she wrote that she had believed in this tool, that it should never have driven a father to end his life, and that this technology had been born in a child’s bedroom, to watch over a fragile life.
Jacques reread that sentence several times.
He did not know whether he forgave her. She had not lied from the beginning. Her fault had not been wanting to harm. She had believed without limit in an imperfect creation, born of man, trained by man, sold by man, then delivered to organizations that no longer knew how to recognize suffering without turning it into a signal.
Kestrel withdrew discreetly. The firm first recommended an independent review, then exited its stake. Simon Vale called Jacques one last time. His voice remained gentle. He spoke of responsibility, political risk, a context that had become impossible.
“Jacques, we have enormous admiration for what you have done. But the media and political pressure is too strong. We can no longer follow you.”
He added that some technologies were right too early.
Jacques listened without answering.
Adrian left first. He wrote a long message explaining that he could no longer work on models whose moral limits now collided with his convictions. Vivian held on six months longer. Then she sold what remained: a few patents and the company’s least controversial models. The brand had become tainted and unusable. She sent Jacques one last message.
“We were not bad enough to fail early.”
Since then, the world had gone on.
It had even accelerated.
People no longer spoke only of agents. The word already seemed old. Machines no longer merely wrote, answered, classified, summarized, or generated images. They entered the world with bodies and arms. They learned by touching, pushing, falling, correcting their gestures. Newspapers now spoke of models capable of representing the world to themselves. Intelligence was no longer described only as the capacity to produce language, but as the capacity to act in an uncertain environment.
Yann Kerguen, whose work Jacques had followed for a long time, had become the face of this new age. Nothing seemed able to stop the growth of his company, Materia. It promised machines capable of reasoning and planning in complex real environments. Investors followed. Governments too. Television panels invited him. Conferences acclaimed him. His phrases, often obscure, were repeated like prophecies.
Materia no longer merely predicted the next word, but the next movement, the next impact, an object’s resistance, the trajectory of a sliding glass, the weight of a door, the fatigue of a fabric, the likely intention of a body in a corridor.
Materia had become the name of the age.
The company had first been received with skepticism, then with fascination, then with that form of retrospective obviousness that markets grant to victors. Its machines spoke less than the others. But they knew how to act. They learned the house, the factory, the hospital, the road, the staircase. They were seen in videos making an omelet, carrying an old man, repairing a leak, sorting waste, or tidying a child’s bedroom with attentive slowness.
Jacques watched all this with unease. The language models of the previous decade had imitated thought through language. The new systems imitated what is found in the speechless animal world: the body’s adjustment to reality.
Thomas was growing up among them.
At school, the children used adaptive tutors. Notebooks had disappeared. Each pupil had a learning companion that tracked progress, recomposed exercises, slowed explanations, invented examples, corrected mistakes. Machines knew how to be patient. They did not sigh. They did not have another child to go and pick up.
One evening in November, Thomas came back from school with a drawing folded in four in the pocket of his coat.
Lyubov was still at the office. Jacques had cooked pasta and cut an apple into quarters. Thomas was eating slowly, distracted by a little mechanical spider moving across the tablet screen. It showed how animals distribute their weight over several legs. When he touched one of the joints, the simulation changed, and the spider fell, then got up differently.
Jacques put his hand over the screen.
“We’re eating.”
Thomas looked up.
“I was watching how it learns.”
“It’s not really learning. It’s a simulation.”
“But it falls and then it knows.”
Jacques remained silent.
The remark was simple. In a way, it was true.
Thomas picked up a piece of pasta with his fingers.
“Papa?”
“Yes.”
“Why do I have to learn?”
Jacques smiled at first, thinking it was a trick to avoid the evening reading.
“Because you’re going to need it.”
“But the machines know.”
“They know a lot of things.”
“More than you?”
The question hurt Jacques.
He wiped a streak of sauce from the table.
“Yes. Much more than me.”
Thomas thought.
“More than Mama?”
“Yes. In many things.”
“Then why am I learning?”
Jacques suddenly felt very tired.
He could have said: to think for yourself.
But Thomas would have asked what thinking meant, and Jacques was no longer certain he could answer without lying.
He could have said: so as not to depend on machines.
But their entire life depended on them. The heating, medical appointments, translations, journeys, shopping, accounts, preliminary diagnoses, homework, book recommendations, pronunciation exercises in Russian. Even the neighbor’s piano was corrected by a patient app.
He could have said: to be free.
But freedom had become too large a word for the kitchen, too philosophical for this sticky table and this child waiting for a real answer.
Thomas pressed on.
“At school, Milo says the machines are going to do everything. Even stories. Even doctors. Even drive buses. Even teach babies.”
Jacques thought fleetingly of Naptio.
He had believed in it too.
“The machines will do many things.”
“Everything?”
“No.”
“Why not?”
Jacques looked at his son. He saw his smooth forehead, his hair slightly stuck down by the rain, the mouth still stained with tomato. He remembered Thomas as an infant, his crumpled face, the tiny weight against his chest. He remembered Sentiens. His chest tightened at the memory of the Liverpool employee’s sentence, written in the letter: “They even knew when I was pretending to be all right.” He remembered the phone vibrating in his hand while Thomas slept behind the door.
“Because there are moments,” he said slowly, “when knowing what will probably happen isn’t enough.”
Thomas frowned.
“Why?”
“Because you have to decide.”
“Machines decide.”
Jacques shook his head.
“They choose among possibilities they’re given. They predict. They calculate. They can even invent paths. But deciding, really deciding, is not just choosing what works best.”
Thomas was looking at him seriously.
Jacques searched for an image.
“Imagine that everyone says you have to move in one direction. The numbers say yes. The machines say yes. The adults say yes because they’re afraid to say no. And then something happens. Someone suffers. Someone falls. A rule that seemed good becomes bad. At that moment, it is not enough to ask what is probable. You have to ask what is just.”
Thomas did not answer.
Jacques went on, more for himself than for the child:
“A machine can learn the rules of the world. It can learn how things fall, how bodies move, how sentences answer one another, how men sometimes lie with their faces. But when the rules break, when a new one has to be invented, when you have to say: we will not do that, even if it works, then you need someone.”
“A human?”
Jacques hesitated.
The word suddenly seemed too vast to him, almost too worn out. Human. It had been put on posters, in charters, in launch speeches, on the homepages of the most inhuman software. Human-centric. Human-in-the-loop. Human oversight. He himself had used those words. He knew how much they could lie.
“Someone who answers,” he said.
Thomas tilted his head.
“Answers what?”
“Not only what. For what he does. For others. For you.”
The child lowered his eyes to his plate. He took a quarter of apple, bit into it, then asked:
“But what if I don’t know?”
Jacques felt something give way inside him.
He recognized that question. It had been his for years. What do you do when you do not know? He had spent his life building systems to reduce that ignorance. Models, reports, tools, scores, signals. He had wanted to know before answering.
“Then you learn,” he said.
“But why?”
Jacques smiled sadly.
“Not to have all the answers. So that you do not always let others answer in your place.”
Thomas seemed disappointed.
“That’s hard.”
“Yes.”
“The machines don’t find it hard.”
“Maybe they don’t find it hard because they don’t know what it costs.”
This time, Thomas did not understand. Jacques saw it. He did not insist.
Later, after the bath, the two of them sat on the living-room rug. Lyubov had sent a message. She would be home late. Thomas had chosen a book with a hedgehog on the cover. Jacques resumed reading at the chapter where they had stopped the day before.
Outside, the rain went on, fine and stubborn. The lights in the apartment buildings opposite came on one by one. In some windows, human silhouettes could be seen passing. In others, only the bluish glow of a screen. A small delivery robot stopped at the curb, waited for a car to pass, then crossed carefully.
Thomas looked through the window.
“It knows how to cross.”
“Yes.”
“Did it learn?”
“Yes.”
“And if there’s someone crying in the middle of the road?”
Jacques raised his eyes.
“Then it will have to stop.”
“Will it know that?”
“Maybe.”
“And if it doesn’t know?”
The question fell into the room with terrible softness.
Jacques thought of the Liverpool employee. Of all those whose signals had been seen, but not heard. Of those whose fear had formed an average, whose distress had become an orange color on a dashboard. He thought of machines growing ever more skillful, of world models, of robots able to grasp a fragile glass, of systems able to anticipate a movement before the human body had even finished it.
“Then,” he said, “someone will have to stop anyway.”
Thomas left the window and came back to him.
“You?”
Jacques did not know what to answer.
He would have liked to say yes with confidence. Yes, I will be there. Yes, I will know. Yes, I will show you the way. But he had failed too much to promise that. He now knew that a father does not possess the world he passes on. He receives it damaged, incomplete, already changed by forces he poorly understands. He cannot always explain. He cannot always protect. Sometimes, he can only not run away.
Thomas climbed onto his knees. He had grown heavy. That weight always surprised him. Jacques put a hand on his back. The child smelled of soap, apple, and the warmth of approaching sleep.
“Papa?”
“Yes.”
“Do you know what I have to do when I’m grown up?”
Jacques closed his eyes for a moment.
He saw again Paris, Stockholm, Singapore, London, the glass offices, the hotel rooms, the bars, the screens, the women he had loved, Lyubov, and Thomas a few hours old.
“No,” he said.
Thomas lifted his head.
Jacques added:
“I don’t know what you will have to do. I don’t know what world you will have before you. The machines will be everywhere. Some will help you. Some will want to think for you. Some will know you very well. They will know what you like, what you fear, what you are going to ask, what you are going to forget. They will know many things before you do.”
Thomas listened to him, motionless.
“But they will not be you.”
His eyes were now almost closed. His breathing was regular. He was on the verge of falling asleep.
Jacques placed his hand on his forehead. His palm covered the little warm space above the eyes, where the skin still seemed to keep a trace of babyhood. He felt life there beneath his hand, fragile and entire.
“They will be able to answer you,” he said. “They will be able to explain the world to you. They will be able to show you the stars, the planets, the bones of dinosaurs, scientific discoveries, the games of children in other countries. They will even be able to teach you how to ask better questions. But there will be moments when no one will be able to answer in your place. Not me. Not them.”
Thomas breathed against him.
“You will be afraid, perhaps. I too will be afraid. But I will be there. I will not always know. I will make mistakes again. But I will stay close to you. And when it is time to enter this world, we will enter it together.”
Thomas said nothing.
Jacques slowly stroked his forehead. At that moment, it seemed to him to contain all that still resisted.
In the street, the delivery robot had disappeared. The windows opposite continued to glow. Farther off, above the rooftops, London spread its web of lights, sensors, screens, and artificial voices. The machines were learning the weight of the world. They were entering houses, schools, hospitals, factories, children’s bedrooms. They were learning to see, to touch, to foresee, to answer.
Thomas fell asleep against him.
Jacques remained still for a long time.
He was no longer thinking about saving man from the machine. That phrase now seemed too large to him, too proud. Man had never been alone with himself. He had always understood himself through his gods, his tools, his clocks, his engines, his computers, and his models. Machines had not come from outside. They had come out of him, out of his impatience, his fear, his genius, his desire to be delivered from himself.
But there was still this.
A sleeping child on his knees.
A hand resting on a forehead.
Lyubov came home later. She opened the door softly and found them like that, in the dark living room, the child against his father, the city’s light on their faces.
She said nothing.
Jacques raised his eyes to her.
He did not try to explain.
She came closer, placed a hand on Thomas’s shoulder, then on Jacques’s. For a few seconds, the three of them remained in that small silent warmth, at the edge of a world that no one yet understood.
Outside, the machines went on learning.
Inside, Jacques held his son.
